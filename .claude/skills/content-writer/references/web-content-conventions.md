# Web Content Conventions

Reference for generating landing pages, web pages, and product pages.
Every guideline here is grounded in conversion research, eye-tracking studies,
and performance data from 2024–2026. Where data conflicts, the more conservative
finding is used.

---

## What the research actually says first

Before structure and copy, three findings shape everything else:

**Mobile is the primary device — but desktop still converts better.**
Mobile accounts for 64–66% of global web traffic in 2025. For ecommerce,
mobile drives 60–73% of traffic. But desktop converts at roughly double
the rate: 4.3% desktop vs. 2.2% mobile on average. The gap exists because
of UX friction — form complexity, small tap targets, slow loads. Design
mobile-first, then verify on desktop. Don't assume traffic share equals
revenue share.

**Speed directly costs money, measurably.**
Every 100ms of load time costs approximately 1% in conversions. Pages
loading under 2 seconds show a 9% bounce rate; those exceeding 5 seconds
hit 38%. For Core Web Vitals (2025): target LCP under 2.5s, INP under
200ms, CLS under 0.1. Only 42% of mobile sites pass all three thresholds —
that gap is a competitive opportunity, not a baseline to match.

**Word count is a correlation, not a cause.**
Google does not rank pages higher for being longer. Pages in positions 1–3
average 2,100–2,500 words, but that's because comprehensive content tends
to better satisfy search intent. A 600-word service page can outrank a
2,500-word article if it answers the query more directly. The right length
is: as long as it takes to satisfy the intent, no longer. The rule is
competitor analysis, not word count targets.

## Content Types

### Landing Pages

**Purpose:** Single goal — convert visitors into leads or customers.
**One CTA per page.** Repeat it multiple times (above fold, after proof,
bottom), but make it the same action every time. Multiple competing CTAs
reduce conversions.
**Length:** Depends on offer complexity and traffic temperature.

- Cold traffic, high-ticket, or complex offers: 1,500–2,500 words
- Warm traffic, simple offer, single action: 500–1,000 words
- Test and measure. There is no universal right length for landing pages.

### Web Pages (informational/service)

**Purpose:** Inform visitors, establish authority, guide toward a next step.
**Length targets:**

- About pages: 400–800 words (humans skim; give them the essentials)
- Service pages: 600–1,200 words (enough to rank, enough to sell)
- Resource / pillar pages: 1,500–3,000+ words (depth earns rankings and links)

**The research on length:** Semrush, Yoast, and Ahrefs all agree — match
the depth of the top-ranking pages for your target keyword, then aim to be
more useful, not just longer. Check competitors before setting a word count.

### Product Pages (ecommerce)

**Purpose:** Remove doubt and complete the transaction.
**Length:**

- Simple, low-price products: 300–600 words (images carry more weight than copy)
- Mid-complexity products: 600–1,200 words
- High-ticket or complex products: 1,200–2,000+ words (longer descriptions
  convert better for expensive or technical products)

**The data point that changes product page strategy:**
Products with 5+ reviews convert 270% better than those with zero reviews
(Spiegel Research Center). For products over $100, 5 reviews increases
purchase likelihood by 380%. Reviews are the highest-ROI element on most
product pages — most stores hide them below the fold, which is backwards.

## Landing Page Structure

Users scan before they read. Eye-tracking research (Nielsen Norman Group)
shows most users follow an F-pattern on text-heavy pages: two horizontal
sweeps across the top, then a vertical scan down the left side. On simpler
layouts — like landing pages — they follow a Z-pattern: top-left to top-right,
diagonal to bottom-left, then bottom-right where the CTA typically sits.

**Implication:** Put your headline, subheadline, and CTA in the natural
scan path. Don't make users hunt for what they're supposed to do.

### 1. Hero Section (above the fold)

The hero must answer two questions in under 3 seconds: "What is this?" and
"What do I get?" A well-written headline can produce up to a 307% increase
in conversions — it's the highest-leverage copy on the page.

**Elements:**

- Headline: outcome-focused, not feature-focused. "Save 10 hours a week
  on reporting" beats "AI-powered analytics dashboard." Keep to 6–12 words.
- Subheadline: who it's for, what makes it different, what happens next.
  2–3 sentences maximum.
- Primary CTA: visible without scrolling. Use first-person copy — "Get My
  Free Trial" converts 90% better than "Get Your Free Trial" (Content Verve
  A/B test). Personalized CTAs convert 202% better than generic ones.
- Trust indicators: client logos for B2B; security badges for ecommerce.
  These work above the fold — they reduce the psychological barrier before
  the first scroll.
- Hero visual: video on landing pages increases conversions up to 86% vs.
  static images. The visual must relate to the offer, not serve as decoration.

**What to cut:** Navigation menus. One study showed removing the primary
nav and placing a form above the fold increased conversions by 336%.
Landing pages are not websites — remove anything that competes with the CTA.

### 2. Problem Section

Make the problem concrete and specific before presenting the solution.
Visitors need to feel recognized before they'll trust a product.

- Name the specific pain, not a general category
- Use language the audience actually uses — pull from reviews, support
  tickets, and sales call transcripts
- Keep to 2–3 short paragraphs; this section earns attention, it doesn't
  spend it
- Avoid statistics that are obviously invented or too precise to be real
  ("73.4% of professionals feel overwhelmed" signals AI copy)

### 3. Solution Section

Present your offer as the transformation from the problem state to the
desired outcome. Outcome language outperforms feature language.

- Lead with what life looks like after using the product
- "Before and after" framing works — give the reader a concrete picture
  of the change
- One visual that represents the solution (screenshot, diagram, or result)
- Keep the copy tightly connected to the specific problem named above

### 4. Features / How It Works

Show the mechanism that produces the outcome. Three to six features is
the right range for most offers. More than six dilutes attention without
adding conversion value.

**Format:** Icon + specific headline + 2–3 sentence description.

**The features vs. benefits distinction that actually matters:**
A feature is what the product does. A benefit is what the user gets.
Most copy stops at features. The high-converting pattern: state the feature,
then immediately state what that means for the user.

- Weak: "Automated follow-up sequences"
- Strong: "Automated follow-up sequences — your team never loses a deal
  to a missed touchpoint again"

### 5. Social Proof Section

Social proof is the highest-leverage trust mechanism available:

- **95%** of consumers read reviews before buying
- **92%** hesitate to buy when no reviews are available
- Adding trust signals near a CTA increases conversions **15–34%**
- **Video testimonials** outperform text testimonials by 80%
- **UGC (customer photos/videos)** increases conversions 29% — and 72%
  of consumers trust customer-submitted photos more than brand photography

**What this means for placement:**
Most sites put social proof below the fold where it gets little traffic.
Put at least one trust signal above the fold (client logos, review count,
or a one-line testimonial). Full testimonials can live further down.

**Testimonial quality signals:**

- Specific outcome with a number ("cut our reporting time from 4 hours to
  40 minutes") beats vague praise ("amazing product, highly recommend")
- Include name, title, company — the more specific the attribution, the
  more credible
- Photos increase credibility further
- Star ratings: 4.2–4.5 is perceived as more authentic than 5.0 (consumers
  distrust perfection)

**For B2B specifically:** 79% of B2B buyers rely on social proof when
making decisions. Case studies with quantified outcomes ("increased pipeline
from $400K to $1.2M in Q3") outperform generic testimonials.

### 6. CTA Section (bottom of page)

The final CTA should not introduce anything new — it should close what
the page opened. Repeat the primary headline's promise, then make the ask.

**CTA copy principles:**

- First person: "Start My Free Trial" not "Start Your Free Trial"
- Specific: "Get My Custom Proposal" not "Submit"
- Reduce friction inline: what happens next? How long does it take?
  "Start in 2 minutes, no credit card required"
- One CTA action. Giving users two choices (free trial / demo) has tested
  both ways — if uncertain, A/B test with your traffic, don't assume

## Web Page Structure

### The inverted pyramid (put this in the guide explicitly)

The inverted pyramid is the most important structural principle for web
pages: most important information first, supporting details second, context
and background last. This matches how users actually read (F-pattern —
they bail early if the opening doesn't earn continued reading).

Most web copy is written in the other order. Professionals write like
lawyers building to a conclusion. Users read like people with options —
they'll leave the moment the page stops delivering value.

### Header

- Page title that matches the search intent and the H1
- Introduction: 1–2 paragraphs covering the core answer or value proposition
- For pages over 1,500 words: include a table of contents (reduces bounce,
  increases scroll depth, and earns sitelinks in Google)

### Main Content

- H2 and H3 subheadings at every major section break — users scan by
  reading subheadings before committing to body text
- Paragraph length: 2–4 sentences maximum. Shorter on mobile.
- Sentences: aim for Flesch-Kincaid grade 7–9 for most B2B/SaaS copy.
  Grade 6 is optimal for highest-converting landing pages across most
  industries. Simpler language is not less professional — it's more usable.
- Bullet points for genuinely list-like content; prose for everything else.
  Don't use bullets to disguise a lack of structure.
- Images or visuals every 300–500 words — not decoration but to anchor
  the scroll and break density
- Internal links to related pages: builds site authority and extends the
  user session

### Supporting Sections

Only include these if they add specific value. Don't add them to hit a word
count or because other sites have them.

- FAQ: include if users ask these questions in search or in sales. Structure
  with FAQ schema for AI citation potential (see SEO section below).
- Related resources: only if the visitor would genuinely benefit from
  continuing — don't cross-link to pad page metrics
- Contact or next step: make the next action explicit at the bottom

## Product Page Structure

### Product Hero

Everything needed to make the purchase decision should be visible without
scrolling on desktop. On mobile, prioritize in this order: product name,
primary image, price, and add-to-cart button.

**Required above the fold:**

- Product name and one-sentence benefit statement (not just a tagline)
- High-quality images: multiple angles, lifestyle shots, and at minimum
  one video. Product images drive 93% of purchase decisions (Nielsen Norman
  Group). This is the single highest-impact investment for most product pages.
- Price — visible and honest. Hidden costs (shipping revealed at checkout)
  are the #1 cause of cart abandonment
- Primary CTA: "Add to Cart" or "Buy Now" — high contrast, thumb-sized
  on mobile
- Key social proof signal: star rating and review count. Put this near
  the CTA, not at the bottom. Products with 11–30 reviews convert 68%
  higher than those with zero (PowerReviews)

### Features and Benefits

The conversion pattern that works: feature → benefit → proof.

- What the feature is
- What it means for the customer
- A number, result, or customer reference that validates the claim

Keep descriptions scannable. Bullet format works here because features
are genuinely parallel and enumerable. Each bullet should stand on its own
— a user scanning bullets, not reading prose, should still understand the
full value of each feature.

### Product Description

Answer the questions a buyer would ask if they could call you:

- What exactly is this?
- Who is it for?
- How does it work?
- What's in the box / included?
- What's the use case most people have?
- What are the common objections?

Longer descriptions convert better for expensive and complex products.
For simple, low-cost products — let the images carry the weight and keep
copy tight.

### Technical Specifications

Use a table. Tables are more scannable than lists for specs.
Include: dimensions, materials, compatibility, what's included, what's
not included, warranty, support.

If the product has technical details that could cause a customer to make
a wrong purchase (wrong size, wrong compatibility), lead with the
dimension or compatibility spec that most often creates returns.

### Reviews Section

**Place it near the product, not at the bottom of the page.**
Most stores put reviews below the fold or at the very bottom where they
get little traffic. This is the single biggest missed opportunity on most
product pages.

High-converting review implementations:

- Lead with a summary (average rating, number of reviews, rating breakdown)
- Feature 3–5 reviews with the highest specificity and relevance
- Enable sorting by "most helpful" or "most recent" — control is a trust signal
- Do not hide negative reviews. Products with 4.2–4.5 stars outconvert
  5.0 stars because consumers trust authentic, imperfect feedback.

### Related Products

Cross-sell and upsell logic that converts:

- "Frequently bought together" outperforms "You might also like" — it
  implies social proof, not editorial judgment
- Show 3–4 products maximum; more creates decision fatigue
- Place below the fold; don't compete with the primary conversion

## Copy Principles

### Write in second person, present tense, for the outcome

"You get 40 hours back every month" is stronger than "Our customers report
saving 40 hours per month." The copy should put the reader in the outcome,
not describe the outcome from a distance.

### Active voice, short sentences

Passive voice and long sentences both increase cognitive load and bounce rate.
Write the way a competent person explains something in conversation, not the
way a lawyer writes a brief.

### Readability targets

- Most landing pages and service pages: grade 7–9 (Flesch-Kincaid)
- High-converting landing pages for broad audiences: grade 6
- Technical documentation or specialist audiences: match the audience

Simpler language reaches more people, even highly educated ones. Expertise
shows through the specificity and accuracy of claims, not through vocabulary.

### Specificity over superlatives

Specifics outperform adjectives in almost every A/B test. The data:

| Weak | Strong |
|------|--------|
| "Fast results" | "Results in 3 business days" |
| "Trusted by thousands" | "Trusted by 4,200 teams" |
| "Significant improvement" | "38% reduction in churn" |
| "Easy to use" | "Set up in under 10 minutes" |

The number doesn't need to be a large round number to be compelling —
specificity signals honesty, which builds trust.

## CTAs: What Actually Works

### The one-CTA rule

Use exactly one CTA per page. Repeat it in multiple locations. Multiple
competing CTAs (Free Trial / Book a Demo / Contact Us) reduce conversions —
users given too many options often choose none.

Exception: ecommerce product pages, where "Add to Cart" (primary) and
"Add to Wishlist" (secondary, lower visual weight) can coexist because
they're not competing goals.

### CTA copy patterns that test well

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| First-person + outcome | "Get My Free Audit" |
| First-person + time-bounded | "Start My Free 14-Day Trial" |
| Frictionless + outcome | "See the Dashboard — No Credit Card" |
| Specific + immediate | "Get the Template Instantly" |

**What to avoid:**

- "Submit" — describes an action, not an outcome
- "Learn More" — no commitment, no value proposition
- "Click Here" — tells them nothing about what happens next
- Generic urgency ("Limited time!") without a real reason — users have
  become immune to false scarcity

### CTA placement that has tested well

1. Above the fold (primary position)
2. After the problem/solution section (when intent is highest)
3. After social proof (when trust is highest)
4. Bottom of page (for users who read the whole thing)

The most common mistake: only placing the CTA at the top and bottom,
with no CTA near social proof where conversion intent peaks.

## Performance Standards

These aren't aspirational. Below-threshold performance directly costs
revenue and search ranking.

| Metric | Target | Impact of missing |
|--------|--------|------------------|
| LCP (Largest Contentful Paint) | Under 2.5s | 32% bounce rate increase per second over threshold |
| INP (Interaction to Next Paint) | Under 200ms | Ranking signal; affects perceived responsiveness |
| CLS (Cumulative Layout Shift) | Under 0.1 | Trust signal; unstable layouts trigger abandonment |
| Mobile load time | Under 3s | 53% of mobile users abandon at 3s+ |
| Image format | WebP | ~30% smaller than JPEG, same quality |

Every 100ms of load time improvement: approximately 1% conversion lift.
For a $1M revenue site, a 500ms improvement is worth ~$5K/month.

**The number that matters most for images:**
Compress all images to under 100KB using WebP. Large images slow 78% of
mobile retail sites. This is almost always the fastest performance win
available.

## Forms

Forms are where landing pages fail most often. 81% of users abandon forms
after starting them. The average form conversion rate is 1–3%; optimized
multi-step forms hit 13.85%.

**Field count:**

- Each additional field reduces conversions by approximately 4.1%
  (HubSpot, 2024)
- Reducing from 11 fields to 4 can increase conversions up to 120%
- The optimal range for B2B lead gen: 3–5 fields (Forrester Research)
- Caveat: fewer fields isn't always better. Removing qualification fields
  sometimes produces more leads but worse quality leads. Test with the
  actual follow-up data, not just submission rate.

**Progressive profiling:**
Collect only the minimum on first interaction. On subsequent form fills,
ask new questions. Build the full picture over multiple touchpoints without
overwhelming any single interaction.

**Mobile form design:**

- Single-column layout only
- Touch-friendly field sizes (minimum 44×44px tap targets)
- Enable autocomplete attributes on all relevant fields
- Real-time inline validation (reduces abandonment by 31%)
- Avoid dropdowns where a text field would work — dropdowns have 15.2%
  higher abandonment on mobile

## SEO Integration

### On-page elements

- H1: one per page, contains primary keyword, matches the title tag intent
- H2–H6: structured hierarchy that a user could follow as an outline
- Primary keyword: naturally present in the first 100 words
- Image alt text: describes what the image shows, not keyword-stuffed
- URL slug: short, lowercase, hyphens between words, keyword present,
  no stop words if they add nothing
- Canonical tag: always set, especially on pages with filters or pagination

### Content for AI citation (2025 onwards)

Beyond traditional SEO, structured content is now a prerequisite for
appearing in AI Overviews, ChatGPT citations, and Bing Copilot answers.

- FAQ sections with FAQ schema markup are cited 2.3x more often in AI
  answers than pages without (even though Google no longer shows FAQ
  rich results for most sites)
- HowTo schema still earns rich results on desktop and is cited in AI
  systems
- Write answers that are complete enough to stand alone — AI extracts
  specific passages, so each paragraph should be able to answer the
  implied question without requiring the surrounding context

### Schema priorities by page type

| Page type | Schema to implement |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Blog posts / articles | Article (required: headline, image, datePublished, author, publisher) |
| Service pages | Service + FAQPage |
| Product pages | Product + AggregateRating (if reviews exist) |
| About page | Organization + Person (for key team members) |
| Any page with steps | HowTo |
| All pages | BreadcrumbList |

## Conversion Optimization

### Above the fold

The rule: everything needed to understand the offer and take the primary
action must be visible without scrolling on desktop. On mobile, at minimum:
headline, sub-headline, and CTA button.

What kills above-fold conversion:

- Navigation menus with multiple options (remove on dedicated landing pages)
- Too many elements competing for attention
- Hero images that take 5+ seconds to load
- Headline that describes the product instead of describing the outcome

### Trust and risk reversal

The psychological barrier to conversion is fear: fear of wasting money,
fear of making the wrong choice, fear of a bad experience. Every trust
signal is fear reduction.

**What works, in rough order of impact:**

1. Specific testimonials with attributed outcomes and real names
2. Star ratings near the CTA (not just in a separate reviews section)
3. Money-back guarantee with a clear, simple claim ("30-day full refund,
   no questions asked")
4. Security badges near checkout or form fields
5. Industry certifications relevant to the audience
6. Client logos (for B2B) — social proof of company peers
7. Customer counts ("Join 4,200 teams") — social proof at scale
8. Press logos ("As seen in Forbes, TechCrunch") — authority transfer

Trust signals near a CTA increase conversions 15–34% (multiple studies).
Placement matters as much as presence.

### Urgency and scarcity (use honestly)

Manufactured urgency (countdown timers that reset, perpetual "limited
time" offers) have been extensively tested and are now net-negative for
brands with even modest repeat traffic. Users recognize it.

Real urgency and scarcity that converts:

- Cohort-based pricing that genuinely closes ("founding member pricing
  ends Friday")
- Actual stock levels ("3 left at this price")
- Time-based offers tied to real events (end of quarter, product launch)
- Waitlists that are actually real

If the urgency isn't real, don't use it. The cost of eroded trust exceeds
the short-term conversion lift.

## Content Length Reference

These are starting points. Always check what's ranking for your target
query first. Length that satisfies intent > length that hits a number.

| Content type | Starting range | When to go longer |
|-------------|---------------|------------------|
| Landing page (simple offer) | 500–1,000 words | Complex offer, cold traffic, high-ticket |
| Landing page (complex/SaaS) | 1,500–2,500 words | High-stakes purchase, multiple objections |
| About page | 400–800 words | Company with interesting story worth telling |
| Service page | 600–1,200 words | Competitive keyword, complex service |
| Pillar / resource page | 2,000–4,000+ words | Target keyword shows long-form in top 10 |
| Product page (simple) | 300–600 words | Price under $50, obvious product |
| Product page (complex) | 800–2,000 words | High-ticket, technical, B2B |

**The metric that matters more than word count:** Does your page answer
the searcher's question better than the pages currently ranking? If yes,
it's the right length. If no, it's not long enough regardless of count.

## Platform Notes

### WordPress

- Use a lightweight theme or custom build — avoid page builders that
  add 300KB of CSS/JS for a landing page
- Optimize Core Web Vitals with WP Rocket or similar for caching/compression
- Yoast or RankMath for SEO tag management
- Test mobile rendering in browser dev tools before publishing

### Shopify

- Use Shopify's native theme system where possible — third-party apps
  add significant load time
- Product schema is automatically generated; verify it in Google's
  Rich Results Test after launch
- Enable Shop Pay and one-click checkout options — mobile-friendly
  payments lift conversions up to 20%

### Webflow

- Strong baseline for Core Web Vitals when built correctly
- CMS collections for blog/resource pages
- Test all interactions on real mobile devices, not just Webflow's preview

### Custom / Next.js / Headless

- Next.js outperforms WordPress by ~20 percentage points on Core Web
  Vitals pass rates (Chrome UX data, 2025)
- Server-side rendering and built-in image optimization are the primary
  reasons — they're worth the implementation overhead for high-traffic pages
- Use semantic HTML5 elements throughout
- ARIA labels on all interactive elements
- Structured data added at the template level, not manually per page

## Quality Checklist

**Copy:**

- [ ] Headline states the outcome, not the feature
- [ ] Value proposition clear within 3 seconds of landing
- [ ] One primary CTA, repeated at each scroll depth milestone
- [ ] CTA copy is first-person and specific ("Get My Report" not "Submit")
- [ ] Social proof includes specific outcomes and attributed names
- [ ] No manufactured urgency that isn't tied to a real deadline

**Structure:**

- [ ] Most important content first (inverted pyramid)
- [ ] H2/H3 subheadings scannable as a standalone outline
- [ ] Paragraphs 2–4 sentences maximum
- [ ] No navigation competing with CTA on landing pages
- [ ] Forms: minimum fields needed for the offer; progressive profiling for rest

**SEO:**

- [ ] H1 matches search intent and title tag
- [ ] Primary keyword in first 100 words
- [ ] All images have descriptive alt text
- [ ] URL slug is short and keyword-relevant
- [ ] Appropriate schema markup validated in Rich Results Test
- [ ] Internal links to related pages included

**Performance:**

- [ ] All images WebP, compressed under 100KB
- [ ] LCP under 2.5s (verify with PageSpeed Insights field data)
- [ ] CLS under 0.1
- [ ] Mobile tested on actual device, not just browser preview
- [ ] No third-party scripts loading on the main thread unnecessarily

**Conversion:**

- [ ] Primary CTA visible above the fold on desktop
- [ ] Primary CTA visible above the fold on mobile (at minimum: headline + button)
- [ ] Trust signal (logo, rating, or testimonial) present above the fold
- [ ] Risk reversal (guarantee, free trial, no credit card) stated near the CTA
- [ ] Form fields limited to what's necessary for this specific offer

## Common Mistakes Worth Naming Specifically

**Using word count as a goal.** Length follows intent. If the top-ranking
pages for your keyword average 900 words, writing 2,500 words won't help
you rank — it'll just make users leave sooner.

**Putting social proof at the bottom.** The highest-intent moment on a
page is after the problem/solution section. That's where testimonials
convert best. Most pages bury them below features or at the very end.

**Landing pages with full site navigation.** Navigation is an exit option.
On a dedicated conversion page, every exit option reduces conversions.
Remove the nav.

**Generic CTAs.** "Learn More" and "Submit" are conversion killers. Name
what the user gets, in the user's words, in first person.

**Forms that ask for more information than is needed to fulfill the offer.**
If you're giving someone a free report, you need their email. Not their
phone number, company size, and annual revenue. Ask for those later.

**Carousel/slider heroes.** Auto-rotating heroes have tested consistently
poorly across industries. Second and third slides are seen by almost nobody.
Use a static hero.

**Mobile pages that are just desktop pages scaled down.** Design for the
mobile interaction pattern (thumb-driven, short sessions, distraction-prone)
separately from desktop. The layout, button sizes, and content hierarchy
should be different.
