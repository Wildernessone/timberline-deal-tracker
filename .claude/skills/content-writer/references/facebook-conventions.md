# Facebook Content Conventions

Reference for generating Facebook posts, page content, and group posts.
Every guideline reflects current algorithm behavior based on Meta's own
transparency data, Sprout Social's 2-billion-engagement dataset, and
platform-level structural changes through 2025–2026.

---

## The platform reality first

Facebook's algorithm changed structurally enough in 2025 that pre-2024
playbooks are actively misleading. Three things have shifted significantly:

**Organic reach from Pages is extremely low — Groups are a different story.**
Facebook Page organic reach averaged 1.37–1.65% of followers in 2025. A Page
with 10,000 followers can expect 100–600 people to see any given post without
paid promotion. Facebook Groups operate under different rules: posts in active
Groups reach 20–40% of members organically. The same 10,000-person audience
in a Group sees 2,000–4,000 people per post — a 10–30x difference. Facebook's
strategic intent is clear: Groups are positioned as the primary organic
distribution channel; Pages are designed to push you toward paid advertising.

**The link penalty is now severe and confirmed by Meta's own data.**
Meta's Q1 2025 transparency report showed 97.3% of the most-viewed posts in
the US contained no external link. Link prevalence in the feed dropped from
9.8% of feed views in 2022 to under 2% in 2025. Internal algorithm research
estimates posts with external links get 70–80% less reach than equivalent
native content. The standard workaround remains: post the link in the first
comment, reference it in the post body ("link in comments").

**40% of the News Feed is now AI-recommended content from people you don't follow.**
This is up from 15–20% in 2024. Facebook is following TikTok's model —
algorithmic discovery over social graph distribution. The implication for
content strategy: content that earns strong early engagement can reach entirely
new audiences without a large follower base. Reels in particular are shown to
non-followers first as an algorithmic test before reaching existing followers.

## Format performance reference

Based on current algorithm weights and Meta's own transparency data:

| Format | Reach strength | Engagement strength | Notes |
|--------|---------------|---------------------|-------|
| **Facebook Reels** | Highest | High | 2–3x more reach than regular video; all videos auto-classified as Reels as of mid-2025 |
| **Photos / image posts** | High | Highest | 35% more feed engagement than text; 44% more than video in-feed |
| **Text-only posts** | Medium | Medium | Works for personal voices; underperforms at scale |
| **Native video (longer)** | High | High | Completion rate is the primary ranking signal |
| **Links in post body** | Very low | Very low | 70–80% reach reduction vs. native content |
| **Links in first comment** | Normal | Normal | Standard workaround that retains reach |

**The format logic:** Photos win for in-feed engagement (likes, comments, saves).
Reels win for reach and discovery (new audiences). Use both. The trap is
defaulting to text-only or link posts when neither performs well organically.

## Post length

The original received wisdom "100–250 characters" overstates the optimal length.
The data is clearer:

- **Under 80 characters:** 66% higher engagement than longer posts
  (HubSpot, Jeff Bullas research confirmed across multiple studies)
- **40–80 characters:** The sweet spot for likes, shares, and comments
- **125 characters:** The "See More" truncation threshold — most users never
  click through
- **Long-form (150–400 words):** Performs when the content is genuinely a
  story, case study, or educational piece with clear structure — not a wall
  of text. Facebook's AI-powered feed rewards content that earns dwell time
  and genuine comment engagement; a compelling story that generates 20 comments
  beats a short post that gets 3 likes.

**The practical rule:** Lead with a short, punchy hook (under 80 characters)
that makes people want to expand. If you have more to say, it's there. If
people stop at the first line, you haven't lost them — you've given them the
core point.

## Post structures

### Pattern 1: Short hook + question (maximum engagement)

```
What's the biggest mistake you see founders make with their first hire?

For us: hiring for "potential" over "can do the job today."

Early-stage startups don't have time to train. You need someone who's done it.

Drop your take 👇
```

### Pattern 2: Story format (educational, earns saves)

```
A founder reached out last week after spending $80K on a platform nobody wanted.

8 months of development. Zero customer conversations. Beautiful code.

What we do differently:
→ 20 customer interviews before writing code
→ Landing page + waitlist validation
→ Paid pilot with 3–5 customers before full build

It's saved every client we've run this with at least 3 months.

Full breakdown in the comments.
```

### Pattern 3: Specific insight + invitation to disagree

```
Your MVP doesn't need real-time features. 🎯

Async + polling works fine until 10K users. Don't optimize for scale
you don't have.

Unpopular opinion? Tell me why I'm wrong 👇
```

### Pattern 4: Numbered list (earns saves)

```
3 questions we ask before writing any code:

1️⃣ Who will pay for this?
2️⃣ What's the smallest version they'd pay for?
3️⃣ Can we build it in 6 weeks?

Saved our clients $2M+ in unnecessary development.

Want the breakdown? Full framework in the comments.
```

### Pattern 5: Behind-the-scenes (builds trust and authenticity)

```
We just wrapped a discovery sprint with a fintech founder.

He wanted 15 features. We convinced him to start with 3.

Framework we used:
1. List every feature
2. Ask: "Would you pay for this product with ONLY this feature?"
3. Build the ones that got a "yes"

Result: 6-week timeline instead of 6 months.

The best development work is often the code you don't write.
```

## Voice on Facebook

Facebook has a distinct register from LinkedIn or blog content:

- **Conversational, not corporate.** Write like you're talking to someone you
  know, not presenting at a conference.
- **Personal stories outperform polished marketing copy.** Behind-the-scenes,
  mistakes made, honest observations — these earn engagement. Branded messaging
  that reads like an ad doesn't.
- **Use "I" and "you."** First and second person create a sense of direct address
  that the algorithm rewards through comments.
- **Opinions earn comments.** Posts that make people want to agree or disagree
  generate the comment-level engagement that Facebook's algorithm treats as its
  highest-quality signal.
- **Fragments are fine.** Perfect grammar signals corporate distance. Casual
  syntax signals authentic voice. "Early-stage startups don't have time to
  train. You need someone who's done it before." — not "Early-stage startups
  require individuals with demonstrated prior experience."

## The engagement signal hierarchy

Facebook's algorithm ranks engagement types by weight. From most to least
valuable:

1. **Saves** (bookmarks) — The highest-quality signal. Posts that users save
   signal high informational value and get extended distribution. Create
   reference-worthy content: frameworks, checklists, how-to guides.
2. **Shares** (especially DM shares) — Video content is shared 1,200% more
   than any other format on Facebook. Shares to personal messages get the
   same boost. Content that makes someone think "I need to send this to X"
   is the highest-performing content on the platform.
3. **Comments** — Depth matters more than count. Longer comment threads signal
   richer engagement. Responding to every comment in the first 60 minutes
   creates thread depth that Facebook treats as a quality post signal.
4. **Reactions** — Loves and Hahas signal stronger engagement than plain Likes
   and correlate with higher reach.
5. **Likes** — Lowest-weight engagement signal. Optimizing for likes is
   optimizing for the wrong thing.

**What this means for content:** Write posts that earn saves (give people
something worth keeping), shares (give people something worth sending),
and comments (give people something worth responding to). Posts that only
earn likes are algorithmically weak.

## Facebook Groups vs. Pages: which to use

| Goal | Use | Why |
|------|-----|-----|
| Organic reach at scale | **Group** | 20–40% of members reached vs. 1–6% for Pages |
| Paid advertising | **Page** | Ads require a Page; Groups can't run ads directly |
| SEO-indexed content | **Page** | Pages are searchable and indexed by Google |
| Community building | **Group** | Conversation, moderation, member trust |
| Formal brand presence | **Page** | Required for business credibility and discovery |

**The hybrid strategy:** Use a Page for SEO-indexed content, paid campaigns,
and official brand presence. Build a Group for organic community engagement
where your actual content gets real reach. A Page with 100,000 followers reaches
2,200 people per post; a Group with 10,000 members reaches 2,000–4,000 per post.
The Group wins despite being 10x smaller.

**Posting to other Groups:** High-value contributions to relevant Groups where
the rules permit sharing work is one of the highest-reach organic tactics on
the platform. A post that reaches 300 people from your Page can reach tens of
thousands from a coordinated multi-group posting strategy. Never spam. Contribute
genuine value to groups before sharing self-promotional content.

## Facebook Reels

As of mid-2025, every video uploaded to Facebook is automatically classified
as a Reel. Short-form video is now Facebook's primary distribution vehicle.

**Why Reels matter for reach:**

- Reels are shown to non-followers first as an algorithmic test — this makes
  them the only format that routinely escapes your existing audience
- Reels get 2–3x more organic reach than regular long-form video posts
- 40%+ of the News Feed is AI-recommended content; Reels are the primary
  format Facebook uses to fill that recommended slot

**Reel format guidance:**

- **Length:** 15–60 seconds earns the largest distribution bonus; under 90
  seconds is the outer limit for sustained reach
- **Completion rate is the primary ranking signal:** A Reel people watch fully
  gets extended distribution; one people abandon at 3 seconds dies
- **Hook in the first 3 seconds:** Most users decide whether to continue in
  the first 3 seconds — open with the most interesting frame, fact, or moment
- **Add captions:** 85% of Facebook videos are watched without sound; captions
  are not optional for mobile audiences
- **Shoot vertically (9:16):** Mobile-first format; horizontal video is
  penalized in the Reels feed
- **Production quality is secondary to hook quality:** Authentically shot,
  high-value content consistently outperforms polished corporate video in Reels
  reach — the algorithm rewards watch time, not production budget

## Links: the rules

**Never put links in the post body if reach matters.**
Meta's own data shows 97.3% of top-performing posts contain no link in the
original message. External links in the post body receive 70–80% less reach
than native content. This is the single biggest reach mistake most brands make.

**The standard workaround:**

1. Post your content without the link
2. Immediately post the first comment with the link
3. Reference the link in the post body: "Full breakdown in the comments" or
   "Case study linked in comments"

**What not to do with links:**

- Link shorteners (Bit.ly etc.) are associated with phishing and get throttled
- Posting links in every post systematically signals to Facebook you're a
  traffic-extraction operation and compounds the penalty
- Never put the same link in multiple posts back-to-back — Facebook detects
  and suppresses this

## Timing

Multiple large-scale studies point to the same general pattern for Facebook.

**Sprout Social (2 billion engagements, 307,000 profiles, Nov 2025–Feb 2026):**
Best time: **Tuesdays and Wednesdays, 12–8 PM** local time.
Mondays and Thursdays also show strong afternoon engagement.

**Hootsuite (2 million+ Facebook posts, 118 countries):**
Best overall time: **5 AM on Tuesdays.** Early morning catches users before
the algorithm gets flooded with the day's posts. Peak window: 5–8 AM.

**The conflict explained:** The data diverges because Sprout Social measures
all engagement including B2C audiences, while Hootsuite skews toward earlier
professional/business content consumption. For B2B content: morning window
(9–11 AM). For B2C/consumer content: midday to afternoon (12–6 PM).

**The practical rule:** Check Meta Business Suite → Insights → Active Times
for your actual audience. Global benchmarks tell you where to start; your
own data tells you where to stay.

**Worst day:** Saturday consistently shows the lowest engagement across almost
all industries and studies.

**Timing and engagement velocity:** Facebook posts stay visible for roughly
5 hours, with 75% of engagement occurring in that window. The first 60 minutes
determine whether the algorithm expands distribution. Post when you can be
present to respond to comments for the first hour.

## First-hour engagement strategy

The first 60 minutes determine your post's reach. What to do:

1. **Post the link in the first comment immediately** (if there's a link)
2. **Respond to every comment within 60 minutes** — create thread depth, not
   just acknowledgment. Ask a follow-up question in your reply.
3. **Respond with length.** Brief "thanks!" replies contribute less to thread
   depth signals than substantive 2–3 sentence responses.
4. **Don't ask colleagues to comment "Great post!"** — Facebook detects
   coordinated inauthentic behavior and suppresses posts where early engagement
   patterns look artificial. Genuine comments from people who read the post
   are the signal.

## Hashtags

Facebook hashtags are a diminishing signal. They're searchable, but Facebook's
algorithm doesn't weight them heavily for reach.

- **0–3 hashtags maximum.** Place at the end of the post or in the first comment.
- Mix one broad (#Startups) with one specific (#MVPDevelopment) when using them.
- Don't use hashtags in every post — selective use is more effective.
- Don't use hashtags for categories that aren't actively followed on Facebook
  (unlike Instagram, where hashtags drive discovery more reliably).

## Engagement-bait: what Facebook explicitly penalizes

Facebook's algorithm has a specific filter for "engagement bait" — posts that
explicitly request engagement actions. These patterns get suppressed:

**Penalized phrases:**

- "Tag someone who needs to see this"
- "Share if you agree"
- "Comment 'YES' if you want the link" / "Drop a 1 if you want this"
- "I'll DM you if you comment"
- "Like this if you agree"
- "Repost this" / "Share this"

The filter is keyword-based and applies to Pages. Don't use these phrases even
if the engagement request feels genuine. Rephrase: "What's your take?" or
"Drop your experience in the comments" instead of "Comment below!"

## Visual content guidelines

### Images

- **Minimum size:** 1200 × 630 pixels (OG standard)
- **Real photos over stock:** Authentic images earn more engagement. Posts with
  real faces earn measurably higher engagement than those without.
- **Text on images:** Keep to a minimum. Heavy text overlay can reduce ad
  eligibility and looks like a promotional post, which Facebook's organic
  algorithm deprioritizes.
- **Carousel posts** (multiple images): Earn saves when the content is worth
  swiping through — frameworks, step-by-step processes, before/after results.

### Video (Reels)

- **Aspect ratio:** 9:16 vertical for Reels; 1:1 square for in-feed video
- **Length:** 15–60 seconds for maximum Reels distribution
- **Captions:** Required for mobile audio-off viewing — use Facebook's
  auto-generated captions and review them for accuracy
- **Completion rate benchmark:** 70%+ for Reels under 15 seconds is strong;
  40%+ for videos over 30 seconds is solid
- **Hook:** The opening frame must earn the next 3 seconds. What's the most
  interesting visual or claim in the content? Open with that.

## What to avoid

### AI tells on Facebook

The conversational register of Facebook makes AI writing patterns especially
visible — they read as corporate when everyone else is personal.

- Em dashes (—) — use periods or line breaks
- "Delve," "landscape," "crucial," "robust," "seamless," "leverage," "foster"
- Perfectly symmetric paragraph structure (all the same length)
- Generic inspirational conclusions ("The future looks bright!")
- Overly formal language that sounds like a press release
- Passive voice ("It was found that...") instead of direct statements
- Throat-clearing openers ("In today's digital landscape...")

### Format mistakes

- **External links in the post body** — the single biggest reach mistake
- **Posting the same content to multiple groups simultaneously** — Facebook
  detects and suppresses this
- **Text-only posts exclusively** — mix in images and Reels for reach
- **Posting and leaving** — the first-hour engagement window determines reach
- **Long hashtag lists** — looks spammy, adds no reach benefit

## Facebook vs. other platforms

**Facebook vs. LinkedIn:**

- More casual tone; LinkedIn rewards professional credibility, Facebook rewards
  authentic personality
- Personal stories work on both; Facebook allows more vulnerability and humor
- Emojis are more accepted on Facebook; LinkedIn skews more restrained
- Facebook's primary engagement metric is comments; LinkedIn weights dwell time
  and follows more heavily

**Facebook vs. Twitter/X:**

- Facebook allows and performs well with longer narrative content; Twitter
  rewards punchy brevity
- Facebook's algorithm weights comments and saves; Twitter's weights reply depth
  and retweet velocity
- Facebook's visual content (photos, Reels) is an active driver of reach;
  Twitter's text posts outperform video
- Facebook has an older core demographic (25–54); Twitter skews younger and
  more professional/media

**Facebook vs. Instagram:**

- Facebook allows more text; Instagram is visual-first
- Links in comments work on Facebook; Instagram limits this to bio
- Facebook Groups have no direct Instagram equivalent and represent Facebook's
  unique strategic advantage
- Instagram Reels and Facebook Reels share the same format but different
  audiences and algorithm contexts

## Shortcode handling

If the user has defined shortcodes in their profile:

- `{{booking_link}}` → Replace with actual URL; place in first comment, not
  post body
- `{{case_study:name}}` → Replace with case study URL; place in first comment
- `{{cta:soft}}` → Facebook CTAs should invite conversation, not transactions:
  "What's your experience with this?" and "Full breakdown in the comments"
  outperform "Book a call at [link]"
