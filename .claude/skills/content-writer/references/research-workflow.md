# Content Research Workflow

A system for finding angles, proof, and audience language before writing anything.
Research is what separates content that gets shared and saved from content that gets scrolled past.

---

## When to run the research workflow

Run this before writing when any of these conditions apply:

- The topic is outside direct experience or the user's documented case studies
- The user wants to validate a claim, find a supporting statistic, or challenge an assumption
- The user provides a URL or article to draw from
- The content covers a new industry, audience, or use case not in the profile
- The target keyword requires understanding what's already ranking and why
- The goal is thought leadership — a contrarian take needs to be grounded in something real

Skip formal research and move straight to writing when:

- The content is personal experience or opinion (the user is the primary source)
- The profile contains a relevant case study that covers the topic directly
- The user has provided all the information needed in the discussion phase

---

## The core principle: steal the audience's language

The most consistently underused research technique is reading what your actual audience writes in public, unprompted.

Reddit, Quora, LinkedIn comments, G2 reviews, App Store reviews, and industry forum threads are where people describe their real problems in their own words — without marketing filters. The sentences people write in those places are the copy that converts. Not because they're polished, but because they're true.

When researching any topic, always look for:

- The exact phrases people use to describe the problem (not how the industry describes it)
- The specific frustrations they express (what's broken, what they've tried, what failed)
- The questions they're still asking after reading the standard advice
- The objections they raise when evaluating solutions

A founder writing "I feel like I'm duct-taping Zapier to a Google Sheet and calling it a CRM" is worth more than ten industry reports. That's the hook, the problem statement, and the pain point in one sentence — and it came from a Reddit thread.

## Step 1: Define the research goal

Before starting, clarify four things:

1. **What's the topic and angle?** Not just "content marketing" — "why most B2B content gets ignored before it's read"
2. **Who is the audience?** Specific: "founders of 5–20 person SaaS companies" not "marketers"
3. **What does this content need to accomplish?** Drive saves, generate leads, build authority, rank in search?
4. **What format is this for?** Blog, LinkedIn post, email, sales page, tweet thread? Each format needs different research depth.

If the angle isn't clear yet, research is partly what creates it. Start with the topic and let the research reveal the angle.

---

## Step 2: Source hierarchy

Not all sources are equal. Use this hierarchy to invest your time correctly.

### Tier 1: Primary sources (use whenever available)

These are the most credible and produce the most differentiated content. AI tools and competitors can't replicate sources from your direct experience.

- **Your own case studies and client outcomes** — specific, attributed, verifiable
- **Customer interviews and call recordings** — exact language, real objections
- **Internal data and metrics** — first-party data that nobody else has
- **Direct observation** — what you've personally seen happen with clients or in the market

When a Tier 1 source exists, lead with it. "We ran this process with 14 clients over 6 months" beats a cited report every time for trust and distinctiveness.

### Tier 2: Secondary sources (the research backbone)

- Original research reports from recognized organizations (CMI, HubSpot State of Marketing, Gartner, Edelman-LinkedIn, etc.)
- Industry-specific data: studies, benchmarks, surveys with named methodology
- Academic research when the topic warrants it
- Recognized thought leaders with documented expertise, not just follower counts
- Trade publications in the specific industry (not general business media)

For each Tier 2 source: note the sample size, the date, the methodology. A 2022 statistic about a fast-moving topic is effectively fiction in 2025–2026.

### Tier 3: Use sparingly, verify aggressively

- General business publications (often cite the same recycled statistics)
- Aggregated listicles (frequently cite each other in circles)
- Social media posts without attribution
- AI-generated summaries (useful for direction, dangerous for specific claims)
- Wikipedia (fine for context, never as a primary cite)

**The 2025–2026 source credibility problem:** AI-generated content floods search results and frequently contains plausible-sounding fabricated statistics. If a statistic can't be traced back to a named study with a methodology, treat it as unverified regardless of how many sites quote it. Verify any number you plan to use by clicking to the actual source — not the article that cited the article.

### E-E-A-T and AI search relevance

Google's E-E-A-T framework (Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, Trustworthiness) now also governs which content AI tools cite when buyers research. Content sourced from verifiable, credentialed primary sources earns citations in AI-generated answers. Unsupported claims don't. When the content is important for search or AI visibility, build it on sources that would survive scrutiny: named authors, named studies, dated and scoped data.

## Step 3: The six things to find

For any content piece, research should produce at least one of each:

1. **The surprising number** — a statistic that challenges what the audience assumes to be true. Specific beats vague: "61% of the B2B buying journey is complete before buyers contact sales" (6sense, 2025) lands; "most buying happens before sales contact" doesn't.

2. **The contrarian take** — what commonly-held belief in this space is wrong, incomplete, or outdated? The best content argues against something. What is the conventional wisdom, and where is it failing people?

3. **The specific example** — a real company, a real outcome, a real number. Named clients where the profile allows, or described clients where it doesn't. "A 12-person fintech startup in Lahore" is more credible than "a startup we worked with." Specificity signals truth.

4. **The audience's language** — the exact phrases your audience uses to describe the problem, the frustration, or the goal. Research this in the places listed below. Then use those phrases in the copy — not a polished marketing version of them.

5. **The common mistake** — what do people in this audience consistently do wrong? This earns saves and shares because it gives readers something to recognize in themselves and correct.

6. **The proof** — what evidence exists that your recommendation works? Internal data, case study outcomes, third-party research, or documented client results. Claims without proof are just opinions dressed as expertise.

## Step 4: Where to look (no-tool research methods)

### For audience language and pain points

**Reddit** — The highest-value free research source for audience language.

- Find subreddits where your target audience congregates (r/startups, r/saastr, r/marketing, r/entrepreneur, etc.)
- Sort by "Top" → "This Year" to find the most resonant posts
- Read comment threads, not just post titles — the comments contain the language
- Look for posts that start with "Why does X never..." or "I'm so frustrated with..." or "Anyone else find that..."
- The upvoted answers to a question show what the community believes is true

**Quora** — Particularly valuable for question-driven content and featured snippets.

- Search your topic and read the answers with the most upvotes
- The comments on answers often contain the objections and follow-up questions
- Quora answers frequently appear in Google's People Also Ask — these questions are research-validated

**G2, Capterra, Trustpilot, App Store reviews** — If writing about a product category.

- Negative reviews at 2–3 stars contain the clearest problem descriptions
- Positive reviews explain the transformation buyers hoped for
- Both give you the exact language of actual buyers

**LinkedIn comments on industry thought leaders** — What people push back on or agree with reveals what's contested vs. settled in your space.

### For finding the angle and the gap

**Google Autocomplete and People Also Ask** — Type your topic into Google and observe:

- Autocomplete shows common modifiers (what people add to the search)
- People Also Ask reveals related questions that indicate what else the audience is wondering
- "Related searches" at the bottom shows adjacent topics

Reading the top 5 SERP results for your target topic tells you what's already been said. Your content needs to be different — a different angle, a different level of specificity, different data, or a direct challenge to what ranks.

**Google Trends** — Compare search term trajectories to see whether interest in a topic is rising or falling. A declining topic is a harder organic bet; a rising one is an opportunity.

**AlsoAsked.com** — Organizes People Also Ask data into clusters. Useful for finding sub-questions within a topic that aren't covered by existing content.

### For validating statistics

Before including any statistic:

1. Click through to the original study, not the article citing it
2. Check the date — data more than 2 years old in a fast-moving field is suspect
3. Check the sample size and methodology
4. Check whether the number is being used correctly (a B2C stat applied to a B2B argument, or a US-only stat applied globally, are both common errors)
5. If you can't find the original source: don't use the number

**Specific search operators that find primary sources:**

- `[topic] report 2024 OR 2025 site:hbr.org OR site:mckinsey.com OR site:gartner.com`
- `[topic] "study found" OR "survey of" OR "analysis of" [number]K OR [number] million`
- `[topic] benchmark 2025 filetype:pdf`

## Step 5: Research by content type

Different formats need different research investment.

### Blog article (800–3,000 words)

**Research depth:** High. This is the format that builds authority over time — it will be indexed, shared, and potentially cited for years.

**What to find:**

- 1 core statistical claim that anchors the piece (primary source, dated)
- 1–2 specific case study examples (internal preferred, third-party verified as backup)
- 3–5 secondary sources that support the framework or argument
- The contrarian angle that differentiates from existing top-ranking content
- The audience language from Reddit or review sources

**SERP check before writing:** Search the primary keyword and read the top 5 results. Note what they all cover (table stakes — cover it). Note what none of them cover (the gap — that's the angle). Note what angle they argue (conventional wisdom — consider challenging it).

**Time investment:** 45–90 minutes of research before writing. Content that took half an hour to research sounds like it.

### LinkedIn post (long-form, 1,200–1,600 characters)

**Research depth:** Medium. One strong insight with proof.

**What to find:**

- 1 specific number or surprising stat
- 1 concrete example (can be from the profile's case studies)
- The contrarian claim that makes someone stop scrolling

**Time investment:** 15–30 minutes. Often, the research is already done — check the profile's case studies and existing content for the insight, then find one external data point to anchor it.

### Twitter / X thread

**Research depth:** Medium. Framework or process with specific steps.

**What to find:**

- The core claim that works as a standalone tweet (hook)
- 1 proof point per major step (case study outcome or specific metric)
- The framework structure that makes the thread actionable

**Time investment:** 20–40 minutes.

### Email newsletter or campaign

**Research depth:** Medium. Email rewards relevance and timeliness.

**What to find:**

- A recent event, trend, or piece of news that makes the topic urgent right now
- 1 internal case study or client story that illustrates the point
- The problem/solution pair that justifies the CTA

**Time investment:** 20–45 minutes.

### Sales page or landing page

**Research depth:** High. Every claim needs to be defensible.

**What to find:**

- The audience's exact language for their problem (Reddit, reviews)
- Specific social proof: named testimonials, attributed case study outcomes
- Competitor positioning gaps (what they don't offer, what they don't solve)
- The objections the audience actually has (not the ones you assume they have)

**Time investment:** 60–120 minutes. Sales copy built on shallow research converts less.

### Content package (blog + social + email from one topic)

**Research depth:** High. Invest once and extract multiple pieces.

**What to find:** Same as blog article, plus:

- 2–3 sub-insights that can each carry a standalone social post
- 1 email hook that's a different angle from the blog headline
- The most shareable statistic for social

**Time investment:** 60–90 minutes upfront, saves writing time across all formats.

## Step 6: The research brief

Before writing, create a brief that captures what you found. It doesn't need to be long — it needs to contain the specific things that will make the content concrete.

```
# Research Brief: [Topic]

Target audience: [Specific description]
Content format: [Blog / LinkedIn / Email / Package]
Angle: [One sentence — what's the contrarian take or unique perspective?]

## Anchor insight
[The most surprising finding — the stat, observation, or contrarian claim that
anchors the whole piece. Include the source and date.]

## Supporting evidence
- [Specific stat or data point with source]
- [Specific stat or data point with source]
- [Example or case study with outcome]

## Audience language
[Exact phrases from Reddit, reviews, or interviews that describe the problem
in the way your audience would say it — not how you'd market it]

## The gap
[What does existing content on this topic miss, get wrong, or understate?
This is the angle.]

## Hook options
1. [Specific stat or surprising claim]
2. [Specific scenario or story opening]
3. [Direct contrarian statement]

## Key CTA
[What should the reader do after reading? Match to profile defaults.]

## Sources
- [URL or citation with date]
- [URL or citation with date]
```

## Step 7: Research quality check

Before moving to writing, verify:

- [ ] **Every claim is specific** — No vague statements ("many companies," "most marketers"). Named numbers, named companies, or "in our experience with X clients over Y months."
- [ ] **Primary source exists for key statistics** — You've clicked through to the actual study, not just the article citing it.
- [ ] **Data is current** — Nothing older than 2 years for fast-moving topics. Nothing older than 3 years for any claim used as a primary anchor.
- [ ] **Audience language is documented** — You have at least 3–5 real phrases your audience uses, from the sources above, not paraphrased.
- [ ] **The gap is identified** — You know what existing content on this topic gets wrong or leaves out.
- [ ] **At least one proof point is internal** — A case study, client outcome, or direct experience (from the profile) that competitors can't replicate.
- [ ] **The contrarian angle is specific** — Not "conventional wisdom is wrong" but "the advice to do X consistently fails because Y, and here's the evidence."

## From research to writing

### The key transformation: make it specific

Raw research contains general facts. Good content contains specific illustrations of those facts.

| Raw research | Transformed for content |
|---|---|
| "Most B2B buyers complete research before contacting sales" | "By the time a buyer emails you for a demo, they've already made a provisional shortlist. Your content either got you on it or didn't." |
| "Customer interviews improve conversion" | "We ran 20 interviews before writing a line of code on this. The product we shipped looked nothing like the one we planned." |
| "Social proof increases conversion" | "The case study we put next to the pricing section doubled our demo-request rate in three weeks." |

The research gives you the truth. The transformation gives you the version that lands.

### Maintaining voice during research

Research-heavy content risks sounding like a report. Prevent this by:

- Reacting to what you find ("This number surprised me." / "Here's why this matters more than people realize.")
- Connecting every data point to the reader's situation specifically ("If you're running a 10-person dev team, this changes how you should think about...")
- Using research to add specificity, then returning to opinion — the writer's voice is the filter, not just the aggregator
- Running the anti-AI checklist after heavy research drafts — research-sourced content is the most prone to sounding assembled rather than written

## Attribution rules

**Direct quotes:** Use quotation marks, cite the source and their role/organization.
Example: "Most buying decisions are made before the buyer contacts sales," says the 6sense 2025 Buyer Experience Report.

**Statistics:** Include the source and year inline.
Example: 61% of the B2B buying journey is complete before buyers contact sales (6sense, 2025).

**Frameworks:** Credit the originator if it's not original.
Example: The PASTOR framework was developed by copywriter Ray Edwards.

**Internal case studies:** Confirm with the profile whether the client can be named, anonymized by type ("a 12-person fintech team"), or is fully confidential.

Do not restate the same statistic in multiple pieces as though it's freshly researched each time. Build a reusable research file for frequently cited numbers — one that includes source, date, methodology notes, and the last time you verified it was still accurate.

---

## Saving research

When research is substantial or reusable, save the brief to `content-writer-output/research/` with the topic name as the filename.

Format: `content-writer-output/research/[topic-slug]-research-brief.md`

This prevents re-researching the same territory, builds a knowledge base for future content, and preserves the source chain for attribution. Saved briefs are also useful for repurposing — a blog article brief often contains enough material for 3–5 social posts that weren't written the first time.
