# Sales Content Conventions

Reference for all sales-oriented content: case studies, testimonials, sales pages,
cold outreach, proposals, product descriptions, and thought leadership.
Every guideline is grounded in current research (2025–2026).

---

## The structural reality of B2B buying in 2026

Before writing a single word of sales content, you need to understand how decisions
actually get made. Almost everything in this file flows from these four facts.

**Fact 1: 80% of deals are decided before first contact.**
6sense's 2025 Buyer Experience Report found that 80% of B2B deals are won by the
vendor the buyer favored before ever contacting sales — and that shortlist forms
during an anonymous, self-directed research phase that most sellers never see.
61% of the buying journey is complete before a buyer fills out a form or speaks
with a rep. The job of content isn't to persuade people on a sales call. It's to
be the obvious choice before one ever happens.

**Fact 2: The buying committee is 13 people, not 6.**
Forrester's 2025 Buyers' Journey Survey found the average B2B purchase involves
13 internal stakeholders plus 9 external participants. The often-cited "6.8
decision-makers" figure is dead. These 13 people have different priorities: the CFO
wants ROI data, the CTO wants security and integration, the end users want usability,
legal wants compliance, procurement wants pricing levers, and the CEO wants strategic
fit. Content that only speaks to one of these personas will be dismissed by the others.
40% of deals stall not because the champion loses faith — but because internal
misalignment kills them. Your content must help your champion sell internally.

**Fact 3: 50%+ of B2B buyers now research using AI tools.**
Redpoint Insights' July 2025 survey of 250 US buyers found that more than 50% of B2B
buyers regularly use ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini to research solutions. Among
C-suite buyers, that number jumps to 67%. AI-referred sessions grew 527% year-over-year
in the first five months of 2025. A vendor that doesn't appear in AI-generated answers
for their category's core buying questions is invisible at the moment shortlists form.

**Fact 4: Trust, not features, closes deals.**
The 2025 Edelman-LinkedIn B2B Thought Leadership Impact Report (2,000 global
professionals) found that trust now carries equal weight to cost and quality for more
than 50% of buyers in purchase decisions. When buyers rank what content establishes
trust: original research (70%) > expert opinions (64%) > peer insights (62%) >
customer testimonials (55%) > case studies (54%). Marketing materials and product
sheets score the lowest. Buyers actively skip vendor hype and seek external proof.

These four facts define the entire content strategy. Every format below exists to
address one or more of them.

## Trust hierarchy: what content types buyers actually trust

Based on Redpoint Insights (250 US B2B buyers, July 2025):

| Rank | Content type | Trust score |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| 1 | Original research (data you generated) | 70% |
| 2 | Expert opinions and analysis | 64% |
| 3 | Peer insights — communities, reviews, referrals | 62% |
| 4 | Customer testimonials (specific, attributed) | 55% |
| 5 | Case studies | 54% |
| 6 | Third-party endorsements | 42% |
| 7 | Authorship / bylines | 22% |
| — | Marketing materials and product sheets | Lowest |

**The implication:** Lead with external proof, not internal claims. A vendor's own
description of their capabilities is the least trusted content type. Case studies
from actual clients rank far higher. Original research — even small-scale studies
with real data — ranks highest. This is the order in which to build your content
library.

Videos are now the most *effective* content type for B2B marketing (58% of B2B
marketers, CMI 2025), overtaking case studies (53%) for the first time. This is
effectiveness at persuasion, separate from trust-building. Use both.

## The dark funnel: content that works where tracking can't see

Most B2B buying happens outside of your marketing stack. Buyers research through
private Slack communities, peer conversations, anonymous browsing, Reddit threads,
podcast episodes, and increasingly, AI tools. These channels leave zero tracking
signals. The "dark funnel" — named for this invisible research phase — now dominates
the early buying journey.

**What this means for content:**

Content that lives inside your marketing automation system (gated assets, email
sequences, tracked downloads) only catches buyers who have already decided to
raise their hand. It misses the 61% of the journey that happens before that.
Content that lives in public, indexable, citable places — LinkedIn posts, thought
leadership articles, Reddit answers, community contributions, AI-cited content —
influences buyers during the anonymous phase when shortlists actually form.

**The dark funnel content model:**

Build content in two layers. Layer 1 is public, ungated, AI-visible, and
community-distributed. It works the dark funnel. Layer 2 is your tracked assets
(lead magnets, demos, proposals) that capture buyers who self-select after the
dark funnel has done its work.

Most agencies invert this — 80% gated, 20% public — and then wonder why their
pipeline is thin. Flip it.

## Thought leadership content

The Edelman-LinkedIn 2025 B2B Thought Leadership Impact Report is the most important
annual study on this topic. Key findings from 2,000 global professionals:

- 71% of hidden decision-makers (finance, legal, compliance, procurement) say
  thought leadership is more effective than conventional marketing at showcasing
  a vendor's capabilities
- 64% trust thought leadership more than marketing materials and product sheets
- 75% of decision-makers, including C-suite, say thought leadership prompted them
  to research a vendor they hadn't previously considered
- 70% of C-suite executives say thought leadership has made them reconsider their
  existing supplier relationship
- 40% of B2B deals stall due to internal misalignment — and high-quality thought
  leadership is the primary tool for reaching the hidden stakeholders who cause that stall

Thought leadership is not a content marketing nicety. It's the primary mechanism for
reaching the 12 people in the buying committee who never filled out a form.

### What qualifies as thought leadership (and what doesn't)

**Does:** Takes a position. Challenges an industry assumption. Shares data nobody
else has. Makes a prediction with a reasoned argument behind it. Disagrees with the
consensus view with specific evidence.

**Doesn't:** Summarizes what everyone already knows. Recaps industry reports without
original analysis. Lists generic tips. Provides a "comprehensive overview" of a topic.
Promotional content disguised as educational.

The bar is: would a senior practitioner in this industry read this and learn something
they didn't know, or have a view they hadn't considered? If not, it's not thought
leadership.

### Thought leadership format guidance

- **Length:** 800–2,000 words for articles. Long enough to make a substantive
  argument; short enough that a senior executive will actually finish it.
- **Lead with the contrarian claim.** Don't bury the point. Open with the
  provocative statement, then support it.
- **Cite specific data.** Original research (even n=50 client surveys) outperforms
  cited secondary research. If you can run a survey, do it.
- **Stake a position.** "We believe X, and here is why" is thought leadership.
  "There are different views on X" is not.
- **Distribute where buyers lurk.** LinkedIn (long-form articles and posts),
  industry publications, relevant Slack communities, Reddit threads in your category.
  AI tools index LinkedIn, Reddit, and high-authority publications most heavily.

## Case studies

69% of B2B marketers call case studies the most effective content type in their
toolkit (CMI 2025). They rank 5th in buyer trust — lower than original research
and peer insights, but still credibly high. The gap between a mediocre case study
and a great one is almost entirely in specificity.

### What kills a case study

Generic language. "We helped a healthcare company improve their operations" is
useless. "We helped an 80-person home health agency in Arizona cut scheduling
errors by 43% in 11 weeks" is a case study that will get forwarded to a buying
committee. Every vague claim destroys trust. Every specific number builds it.

Proposals viewed by 3+ stakeholders within the first 5 days of sending are 1.9x
more likely to be accepted (Qwilr, 1M+ proposal analysis). The same principle
applies to case studies: if the story is specific enough, the champion forwards it.

### Title formula

State the result first, not the process.

Good: "How a 12-Person SaaS Team Cut Development Costs by $180K in One Quarter"
Bad: "A Case Study in Agile Development Optimization"

Include: client type/size (if not named), the specific result, the timeframe.
Numbers in headlines get read. Vague phrases get skipped.

### Structure

**Executive summary (3–4 sentences)**
The reader may not get further. State: who the client was, what their problem
was, what you did, and what specifically changed. Include the headline metric.

**The problem (150–250 words)**
Don't start with "Client X needed a solution." Start with the situation on the
ground — what was breaking, what it was costing them, what they'd already tried.
Quantify the problem wherever possible: "Two developers were spending 15 hours a
week manually reconciling data" is more credible than "they had an inefficiency."
Name the emotional cost too: urgency, pressure, frustration. This is where
the reader either sees their own situation or doesn't.

**Why they chose you (100–150 words)**
This section earns trust by showing you were selected over alternatives, not
handed a captive client. What was the selection process? What convinced them?
What were they skeptical about? This is also where you name any objections they
had and how those were resolved — which preemptively handles the same objections
your next reader has.

**The approach (200–350 words)**
Your methodology, not your marketing. Be specific about what you actually did,
in what sequence, and why. Vague descriptions of "our proprietary process" earn
nothing. "We ran 18 customer interviews in the first two weeks to map the actual
workflow, then identified three critical failure points the client hadn't noticed"
is credible. Include the timeline. Name key decisions.

**Results (200–300 words)**
Lead with the biggest win. State it specifically: the number, the timeframe,
compared to what baseline. Then secondary results. Then unexpected wins (the
things that improved that weren't the original goal — these are often the most
persuasive because they weren't engineered).

The format that works: "[Metric] improved from [before] to [after] in [timeframe],
representing [ROI or business impact]."

**Client quote**
One direct quote, attributed to a named person with their title and company.
Not "great to work with" — that's useless. The quote should speak to either the
results or the experience of the implementation. Specific beats generic: "They
identified a data problem in week two that we'd been living with for three years"
is a quote that gets forwarded. "Highly recommend" is a quote that gets skipped.

**CTA**
Relevant to the use case in the case study. If the case study is about a SaaS
team, the CTA should be "see if this fits your team" — not a generic "contact us."
Match the offer to the reader's situation.

### CITABLE framework: making case studies GEO-ready

For a case study to be cited by AI tools (and shared in dark funnel channels),
it needs to be:

- **C**oncrete: specific numbers, named industry, named timeframe
- **I**ndexable: published publicly, not gated
- **T**itled for the query: include terms buyers search for ("software development
  cost reduction" not "our approach to efficiency")
- **A**ttributed: named client or clearly identified company type with size
- **B**rief in summary: include a 2–3 sentence summary AI tools can cite directly
- **L**inked: share on LinkedIn and relevant industry sites where AI crawls
- **E**xpert-framed: include your analysis of why the result happened, not just that it did

## Testimonials

Video testimonials drive an 80% conversion lift over text-only reviews (The DVI Group,
confirmed across multiple 2025 studies). Text testimonials still matter — but the
hierarchy is: video > specific text > generic text. "Highly recommend" moves nothing.

### The anatomy of a testimonial that converts

The reader is asking one question: "Did this work for someone like me?" Every element
of the testimonial should answer that question.

**The before state** — What was the specific problem? Not "we needed to improve our
process." Specific: "We were losing 6 hours a week to manual data entry."

**The credibility of the hesitation** — What were they worried about before starting?
This is the most underused element of testimonials. A testimonial that says "I was
skeptical it could work for a company our size" and then shows the result is 10x more
persuasive than one that reads as frictionless.

**The specific result** — A number. A timeframe. "We saved $40K in Q1" beats "we
saved a lot of money." Products with 4.2–4.5 star ratings outconvert 5.0 perfect
scores in most markets (social proof research consistently shows this). Perfect
testimonials read as fabricated. Specific, slightly-imperfect testimonials read as real.

**The recommendation** — Written specifically for someone in a similar situation:
"If you're a 20–50 person SaaS team dealing with X, this is worth a serious look."

### Short testimonial (50–100 words)

```
"[Specific problem in their own words]. [What changed and by how much]. [Who they'd
recommend this to specifically]."
— [First name Last name], [Title], [Company name]
```

Example done right:

```
"We were spending 3 days every month reconciling invoices manually. After 6 weeks,
that dropped to 4 hours. I was skeptical it would integrate with our existing stack —
it did on day one. Worth it for any professional services firm billing over $50K/month."
— Marcus Chen, COO, Thornfield Consulting
```

### Video testimonial script (60–90 seconds)

The script should be a guide, not a script. Coached but unscripted testimonials
read as authentic. Scripted testimonials read as fake.

Structure:

1. **Who they are** (5 sec) — Name, title, company, one sentence on what the
   company does
2. **The situation before** (15 sec) — Specific problem, its impact, what they'd
   tried
3. **The turning point** (10 sec) — Why they chose you, what convinced them
4. **What happened** (30 sec) — The specific results, in their own language,
   with numbers if possible
5. **Who else should do this** (15 sec) — Their recommendation, framed for a
   specific audience

Authenticity beats production. A phone-shot, genuine testimonial outperforms a
studio-produced, scripted one in conversion studies. Prioritize getting it captured
over making it polished.

## Social proof: placement and format

Social proof near CTAs lifts conversion 15–34% (web content research, multiple
sources). Strategic placement matters more than quantity.

**Highest-impact placement locations:**

- Immediately below the hero headline (reduces bounce before the page is read)
- Adjacent to pricing (the highest-friction decision moment)
- Near every primary CTA button (immediately before the click)
- At the bottom before the final CTA (conviction before commitment)

**Format hierarchy by conversion impact:**

1. Video testimonials (80% conversion lift over text)
2. Specific text testimonials with attributed name, title, and company
3. Aggregate review scores with volume ("4.8 from 340 reviews")
4. Logo walls from recognizable clients (borrowed authority)
5. Usage statistics ("1,200 teams use this")
6. Press mentions and awards

**3–5 high-quality, specific testimonials outperform 20 generic ones.** Volume
social proof (star ratings, client counts) works for trust signals. Narrative
social proof (testimonials) works for objection handling. Use both, for different jobs.

## The sales page

Great sales copy is not about clever writing. It's about understanding your buyer's
desired outcome, their fears, their objections, and their language — then reflecting
them back in a sequence that builds conviction and removes resistance.

The validated page sequence (research-backed across high-converting pages):

1. **Headline** — Desired outcome, not product name. "Build and launch your MVP
   in 8 weeks" beats "Custom Software Development Services."
2. **Subheadline** — For whom, with what timeframe or constraint addressed.
   "For funded startups that can't afford 6 months of guesswork."
3. **Opening story** — The customer's current situation. Not your company's story.
   Start in their world, not yours.
4. **Credibility hook** — Shortest possible statement of why you can deliver this.
   Not a history of the company. One sentence of proof.
5. **What they get** — Specific, concrete deliverables. Not "a comprehensive
   solution." Exactly what happens, in what sequence, over what timeline.
6. **Social proof** — Case study outcomes and testimonials here, before pricing.
   This is where objections form; this is where proof must appear.
7. **How it works** — The process, step by step. Buying decisions stall when
   buyers can't visualize implementation. Remove that uncertainty explicitly.
8. **More testimonials** — Especially any that address the specific objection
   they have at this point (cost, timeline, whether it works for their situation).
9. **Offer and pricing** — Clear, specific, anchored. See pricing section below.
10. **Guarantee** — Risk reversal. Address the fear of being wrong.
11. **Urgency/scarcity** — Only if real and verifiable. Fake urgency erodes trust
    permanently; real constraints (limited capacity, deadline-bound pricing) convert.
12. **Final CTA** — Restate the core transformation. One clear action.

### Headlines

The headline is responsible for whether the page gets read at all. Tested approaches
in order of documented performance:

- **Outcome + timeframe:** "Get your custom MVP shipped in 8 weeks"
- **Problem statement:** "You're losing 10 hours a week to manual data entry"
- **Specific transformation:** "From prototype to production-ready in 6 weeks"
- **Who it's for + outcome:** "For 5-50 person startups ready to stop rebuilding"

What to avoid: brand names in headlines, clever wordplay that obscures the offer,
generic claims ("quality" "excellence" "innovation").

First-person CTAs outperform second-person by 90% in A/B testing data. "Start my
project" outperforms "Start your project." Apply this to every button.

### The problem section

This is where you demonstrate you understand the reader's world better than they
expected. Two principles:

**Loss framing beats gain framing.** Kahneman and Tversky's foundational research
found losses are weighted roughly 2x as strongly as equivalent gains in decision-
making. "You're losing $8,000/month to this problem" converts more strongly than
"You could gain $8,000/month." Loss framing is particularly effective when paired
with a clear, credible solution and when the loss is real and verifiable. Without
the solution, loss framing causes paralysis. With it, it drives action.

**Mirror their language, not yours.** The words your buyers use in sales calls,
in reviews, in community forums — use those words exactly. "We help founders avoid
building something nobody wants" lands for a startup founder in a way "we optimize
product-market fit" does not. Source language from customer interviews, closed-won
call recordings, and competitor review sites.

### Objection handling

Every unaddressed objection is a lost sale. The five objections that kill most
B2B deals, with the content response for each:

**"It's too expensive"**
Don't discount — contextualize. Break the cost down to a daily or per-user rate.
Show the cost of the alternative (the problem status quo). Show ROI: "If this saves
you 10 hours/week at your team's billing rate, it pays for itself in week 3."
Payment plans and staged pricing lower friction without reducing perceived value.

**"I need to think about it"**
This is almost always a hidden objection. The real concern is one of: they don't
fully believe it works, they're worried about the implementation, they can't get
internal buy-in, or they don't trust the vendor. Surface the real concern with
specific FAQ content or a "most common hesitations" section that names these directly.
Real urgency (capacity limits, pricing deadlines) creates action; fake urgency
creates distrust.

**"I'm not sure it works for my situation"**
Case studies segmented by company size, industry, or use case. Not one generic case
study — the one that matches the reader's specific situation. If you've worked with
5-person teams and 500-person enterprises, you need separate proof for each.

**"I don't have time for implementation"**
Show the process step by step. Quantify the time commitment from their side.
"Your team needs 2 hours in week one for the discovery call and document handoff.
After that, we handle everything." Specificity removes the fear of unknown effort.

**"I need approval"**
Give them what they need to sell internally. An ROI one-pager. A risk summary.
A competitor comparison. A FAQ for their CFO. The deal that stalls at "I need
to check with my boss" is a deal where your champion doesn't have the content
to advocate for you in rooms you can't enter.

## Cold outreach content

### Cold email

Benchmarks from large-scale studies (2025):

- Average cold email open rate: 27.7% (inflated by Apple MPP; track reply rate
  as primary metric)
- Good open rate target: 35–50% with tight ICP and warmed infrastructure
- Average reply rate: 5.1% (positive reply rate is what matters; a 5% reply rate
  with 70% positive replies beats a 10% rate with 20% positive replies)
- Timeline-based hooks achieve 10.01% reply rates vs. 4.3% for problem-based hooks
  — the largest performance gap by hook type in the research
- Personalized subject lines hit 46% open rate vs. 35% without personalization

**Subject line rules (from 36M+ email study):**

- Under 33 characters — front-load value, displays fully on mobile
- 2–4 word personalized question hits 46% open rate, the highest tested structure
- Personalize with at least two attributes (name + company challenge) — lifts
  open rates 14% over single-attribute personalization
- Send to segments of 50 or fewer — sequences targeting 21–50 recipients hit 6.2%
  reply rate; blasts to 500+ drop to 2.4%
- Avoid: "Free," "Limited time," "Act now," "Guarantee," "Click here" — spam
  triggers regardless of content quality
- Spam trigger words kill deliverability before the copy gets a chance

**Email body structure (what converts):**

Paragraph 1: One specific observation about them. Not generic ("I noticed your
company") — specific ("I saw the case study you published on reducing SaaS churn
last month"). This signals you're a real person who did real research.

Paragraph 2: The bridge — connect their situation to a result you've produced
for someone similar. Not your capabilities. A specific outcome: "We worked with
a similarly sized fintech team in Q4 and helped them cut their release cycle from
3 weeks to 5 days."

Paragraph 3: The smallest possible ask. Not "book a 30-minute demo." "Would it
be worth a 10-minute call to see if this applies to your setup?"

**Total: 75–125 words.** Long emails don't get read. Short emails that feel personal
do. Every word past 125 is working against you.

**The 3-7-7 follow-up cadence:**
Research from 11M+ email studies shows sending follow-ups on Day 0, Day 3, Day 10,
and Day 17 captures 93% of total replies by Day 10. Follow-ups beyond Day 17 yield
diminishing returns. Each follow-up should add new information or a new angle —
not just "following up on my previous email."

80% of sales happen after 5+ follow-ups (HubSpot). Most outreach stops after 1–2.
The gap between persistence and annoyance is: persistence adds value, annoyance
repeats the same message.

### LinkedIn outreach

LinkedIn generates 80% of all B2B social media leads. The platform's reply rate
data (2025–2026):

- Personalized connection requests: 35–50% acceptance rate; subsequent messages
  hit 12–18% reply rate
- Cold InMail: 10–25% open rate, 3–8% reply rate
- Connection requests warmed by prior post engagement: 40–60% acceptance rate
- Outreach tied to recent activity (promotion, new role, webinar attendance):
  +32% response rate boost (Sales Navigator data)

**The approach that outperforms:**

Engage before connecting. Like and leave a substantive comment on their post.
Then send a connection request that references the post specifically (under 200
characters — concise notes outperform lengthy ones). Once connected, send a short
non-salesy message that adds one observation and makes a low-friction ask.
Sequenced follow-up messages spaced 2–5 business days apart improve conversions
by 49% over one-off attempts.

**What not to do:**
Pitch on first contact. This is universally called the "pitch slap" in the community
and generates negative sentiment that compounds across your network. The first
LinkedIn message should start a conversation, not close a deal.

**Message length:** Connection note under 200 characters. First message after
connection under 150 words. Keep the ask minimal: "Would it be worth a quick 10
minutes?" outperforms "I'd love to schedule a 45-minute discovery call."

## Proposals

**From Qwilr's analysis of 1M+ proposals:**

- Proposals viewed by 3+ stakeholders in the first 5 days are 1.9x more likely
  to be accepted
- Winning proposals are acted on within 2 days of sending on average
- Deals where prospects get answers to questions within 4 hours have 35% higher
  close rates than those with 24-hour response times
- Executive summaries under 250 words win 32% more deals
- Average RFP win rate: 45% (2025, highest in 5 years)

**The proposal is champion-enablement content.**
The person who requested the proposal is rarely the only one reading it. They're
forwarding it to 12 other people. Write for every reader. The executive summary
should satisfy the CEO. The scope section should satisfy the implementation team.
The pricing breakdown should satisfy the CFO. The timeline should satisfy
procurement. Every section has a different audience.

### Proposal structure

**Executive summary (under 250 words)**
State the problem you're solving, your proposed approach in one sentence, the
expected outcome, and the investment. No company history. No methodology explanation.
The reader will know if they want to continue.

**The situation as you understand it**
Demonstrate you listened. Restate their problem in their language. Include any
details from the discovery call that show you understood the nuance of their situation.
This is the section that loses deals when it's generic ("you need a software solution")
and wins them when it's specific ("your team is manually reconciling 3 data sources
every Friday and it's causing invoice delays that impact cash flow").

**Proposed approach**
What you'll do, in what sequence, over what timeline. Specific milestones.
Clear deliverables at each stage. Name what they need to provide and when —
this signals you've done this before and have a process.

**Evidence of fit**
The case study or outcome that is closest to their specific situation. Not your
best case study — the most relevant one. A 10-person startup client doesn't need
to see what you did for a Fortune 500. They need to see what you did for a 10-person
startup.

**Investment**
Tied to outcomes, not features. Not "X hours at Y rate." "The investment covers
[specific deliverables] by [specific date], producing [expected outcome]. The cost
to build this internally or through an alternative approach is approximately $X."
Anchor pricing against the alternative, not against your cost.

**Risk reversal**
What happens if it doesn't work? What's your guarantee? A clear commitment reduces
the perceived risk of signing. Name your process for scope changes, timeline shifts,
and quality issues — the things buyers fear but rarely ask about.

**Clear next step**
Not "let me know if you have questions." One specific action: "Sign by [date] to
secure [specific slot / pricing / timeline]." If the urgency is real, state it.
If it isn't, don't manufacture it.

## Product descriptions

**The 8-second rule:** Nielsen Norman Group eye-tracking studies found the average
visitor spends less than 8 seconds on a product page before deciding to stay or
bounce. Above-the-fold content must communicate value immediately. A rewritten
product description can boost conversion 20–35% in A/B tests.

### Structure

**Hook (first 50–75 words)**
Not a feature list. The transformation. What does the buyer's life look like after?
What specific problem disappears? Lead with the outcome, not the mechanism.

Loss framing often outperforms benefit framing here: "Stop losing 3 hours every
week to [problem]" frequently converts better than "Save 3 hours every week with
[product]" — because it makes inaction feel costly.

**For whom (explicit)**
Name your buyer. "Built for 5–50 person development teams running sprints" is more
persuasive than "designed for teams of all sizes." Specificity creates identification.
Buyers who see themselves in the description feel the product was made for them.

**Features + benefits in paired format**
Each feature needs its "so what." Not just "automated reconciliation" — "automated
reconciliation, so your team stops spending Friday afternoons on spreadsheets."
Limit to 5–8 features. More than 8 signals poor curation.

**Social proof (within the description)**
One specific quote or metric near the features section. "12 teams used this to
cut their monthly reconciliation from 8 hours to 45 minutes" is more persuasive
placed within the description than in a separate reviews section.

**CTA**
First-person. Action-specific. "Start my free trial" outperforms "Get started."
"Book my demo" outperforms "Request a demo." The copy on the button should state
what the buyer is getting, not what you're doing.

## Sales copy principles: the fundamentals

### Copywriting frameworks

**PAS (Problem-Agitate-Solution)** — The highest-performing structure for cold
outreach and email. State the problem the buyer recognizes, deepen the pain by
showing what it's costing them (lost time, money, opportunity, competitive position),
then present the solution. Works because it mirrors the emotional journey buyers
are already on.

**PASTOR (Ray Edwards)** — Extended version of PAS for longer sales content:
Person/Problem/Pain → Amplify → Story/Solution → Transformation → Offer → Response.
Most effective for sales pages where the product is solving a deeply felt problem.

**SCQA (Barbara Minto / McKinsey Pyramid Principle)** — Situation → Complication →
Question → Answer. The structure of every strong executive communication and B2B
sales proposal. Respects the reader's intelligence and time.

**AIDA** — Attention → Interest → Desire → Action. Classic framework, well-documented,
carries template fatigue risk. Use as a diagnostic to check that all four elements
are present, not as a rigid writing structure.

**The 4 Ps** — Picture → Promise → Prove → Push. Useful for ads and short-form
sales content. Paint the desired outcome, make the specific promise, prove it with
evidence, push to action.

### Psychological principles (backed by research)

**Loss aversion is not optional.**
Losses are weighted roughly 2x as strongly as equivalent gains in decision-making
(Kahneman & Tversky, validated across decades of behavioral economics research).
"You're losing $8,000/month" hits harder than "you could gain $8,000/month" —
not because of clever wordplay, but because of how the brain processes loss signals.
Every sales page, email, and proposal should have at least one piece of loss framing
that is real, verifiable, and paired immediately with the solution. Loss framing
without a solution creates paralysis, not action.

**Urgency and scarcity: real vs. fake.**
Real, verifiable constraints convert. Fake urgency — countdown timers on evergreen
offers, "only 3 left" on unlimited digital products — erodes trust the moment it's
recognized, and buyers in 2025–2026 recognize it immediately. The rule: if the
urgency is genuine (limited capacity, a real deadline, time-based pricing), state
it and explain why it's real. If it isn't, don't manufacture it.

**Social proof must be specific to convert.**
Generic testimonials ("great to work with!") have almost no conversion impact.
Specific testimonials that name the problem, the result, the timeframe, and the
type of buyer who benefited — those create identification and drive action. The
question a reader asks is "did this work for someone like me?" Generic social proof
can't answer it. Specific social proof can.

**Reciprocity builds pipeline in the dark funnel.**
Give value before asking for anything. Free frameworks, original research, honest
analysis of common mistakes — content that helps buyers whether or not they buy
from you builds the trust that moves deals forward in the anonymous research phase.
This is not altruism; it's the documented mechanism of trust-building in markets
where buyers self-educate before first contact.

**Cognitive ease reduces friction.**
Buyers who have to work hard to understand the offer abandon it. Short sentences.
Plain language. No jargon unless the buyer uses it themselves. Every complex idea
explained in the simplest possible terms. The mental effort to understand your
copy competes with the mental effort to evaluate your offer — reduce the former
to make room for the latter.

## GEO: making sales content visible to AI buyers

50%+ of B2B buyers use AI tools to research vendors before contacting sales.
A vendor not cited in AI-generated answers for their category's buying questions
is invisible to those buyers.

GEO (Generative Engine Optimization) is the practice of structuring content to
appear in AI-generated answers. Key findings:

- Content with proper schema markup shows 30–40% higher AI visibility
- AI-referred sessions jumped 527% year-over-year in the first 5 months of 2025
- 32.5% of AI citations come from comparison articles (Princeton/Georgia Tech research)
- Google-page-1 rankings and AI citations now overlap less than 20% — traditional
  SEO and GEO require different strategies

### GEO principles for sales content

**Answer the question directly, first.** AI tools extract direct answers to
specific queries. Content that buries the answer in setup text is less likely to
be cited. Lead with the answer. Follow with context and evidence.

**Name your category and use case explicitly.** "We help B2B SaaS companies with
5–50 employees reduce time-to-ship" is more AI-citable than "we build custom
software." AI tools need specific entity associations to cite your brand for
specific queries.

**Include comparison content.** Comparison articles ("X vs. Y" and "Best [category]
for [use case]") account for 32.5% of AI citations. If you build software, a page
comparing approaches to building custom software will get cited in AI answers
more than a features page will.

**Publish publicly, ungate your best content.** AI crawlers can't cite gated
content. Your most valuable thought leadership — the content that earns dark funnel
trust — should be publicly available and indexable.

**Use specific numbers and named outcomes.** AI tools prefer citing specific,
attributable claims over vague ones. "Cut reconciliation time by 43% in 11 weeks"
will be cited. "Improved efficiency" will not.

**Build presence on platforms AI crawls.** Semrush data from January 2026 shows
Reddit and LinkedIn are the two most cited domains across ChatGPT, Perplexity,
and Google AI Mode. Thoughtful contributions to relevant discussions on both
platforms build the external mention graph that AI tools use to assess authority.

## Content for the buying committee: one deal, multiple audiences

With 13 stakeholders in the average B2B deal, single-audience content leaves most
of the buying committee unaddressed. The champion who loves you still has to get
12 other people to agree.

**Content by stakeholder role:**

| Stakeholder | Primary concern | Content they need |
|-------------|----------------|-------------------|
| **CEO / Founder** | Strategic fit, competitive position | Thought leadership, vision alignment |
| **CFO** | ROI, total cost, financial risk | ROI calculator, cost comparison, payment terms |
| **CTO / Technical lead** | Integration, security, architecture | Technical documentation, security overview |
| **End users** | Usability, learning curve, daily workflow | Demo, UI walkthrough, onboarding preview |
| **Procurement** | Pricing, contract terms, vendor stability | Pricing breakdown, company credentials |
| **Legal** | Compliance, liability, IP | Security certifications, contract templates |
| **Champion / Sponsor** | Internal credibility, ability to advocate | Talking points, ROI one-pager, case study |

The champion rarely controls the deal. They carry it. Give them the content to do
that job: an ROI summary they can forward, a one-page objection handler for their
CFO, a technical FAQ for their IT team. If you don't produce this content, they'll
produce a worse version of it themselves — or they'll fail to make the case and
you'll lose a deal that was already won.

## The sales funnel: what content to create, when

### Top of funnel (dark funnel / anonymous research)

**Goal:** Be in the consideration set before buyers raise their hand.

**Content types:**

- Thought leadership articles (publish publicly, on LinkedIn, in industry publications)
- Original research (even small surveys with real data outperform secondary citations)
- Educational frameworks (the tools buyers use to think about the problem you solve)
- Comparison content ("build vs. buy," "agency vs. freelancer," "approach X vs. Y")
- Community contributions (Reddit, Slack communities, LinkedIn discussions)

**Tone:** Genuinely useful. No pitch. No product mention unless contextually natural.
The test: would you share this with a smart colleague who doesn't know your product exists?

### Middle of funnel (consideration, evaluation)

**Goal:** Be the most credible option when they're actively comparing vendors.

**Content types:**

- Case studies (segmented by industry, company size, and use case)
- Video testimonials (for the specific problems you solve)
- Webinars with substantive content (not product demos disguised as education)
- ROI calculators and tools (interactive, personalized)
- Comparison pages (honest "us vs. alternatives" content — buyers trust this more
  than one-sided product pages)

**Tone:** Evidence-based. Show specific outcomes. Anticipate objections and address
them directly. Let the results do the persuading.

### Bottom of funnel (decision, close)

**Goal:** Remove the last obstacles to saying yes.

**Content types:**

- Sales pages with the full validation sequence
- Proposals (built for champion enablement, not just for the decision-maker)
- FAQ content for specific objections
- Risk reversal (guarantees, pilot structures, phased commitments)
- Stakeholder-specific content (CFO summary, technical overview, compliance FAQ)

**Tone:** Direct. Specific. Address the fear of being wrong more than the
excitement of being right. The buyer at this stage already wants to say yes —
they need permission to.

## Quality checklist for all sales content

Every piece of sales content should answer these questions before publishing:

**The buyer test:**

- [ ] Does the opening sentence speak to a situation the buyer actually recognizes?
- [ ] Is the primary benefit stated specifically (with a number or concrete outcome)?
- [ ] Are the objections a buyer at this stage would have addressed explicitly?
- [ ] Does the buyer know exactly what happens next and what it costs them in time/money?
- [ ] Is there social proof from someone who looks like this buyer?

**The committee test:**

- [ ] If this gets forwarded to the CFO, does it have the ROI data they need?
- [ ] If the CTO sees it, does it address integration and security?
- [ ] Does the champion have what they need to advocate internally?

**The trust test:**

- [ ] Is every claim specific and verifiable (no vague superlatives)?
- [ ] Is the social proof attributed to named people with titles and companies?
- [ ] Is the urgency/scarcity real and explained?
- [ ] Does the content avoid making the reader feel manipulated?

**The AI visibility test:**

- [ ] Is this published publicly (not gated)?
- [ ] Does it directly answer a question a buyer would ask an AI tool?
- [ ] Are specific outcomes, named industries, and company types mentioned?
- [ ] Is there a 2–3 sentence summary at the top that can be cited independently?

**The AI-writing test:**
Apply the anti-AI checklist from anti-ai-checklist.md. Sales content with obvious
AI tells (em dashes, negative parallelism, rule-of-three, generic significance
inflation) signals to sophisticated buyers that the vendor is operating on autopilot.
In a market where trust is the primary buying criterion, that's a deal-breaker.
Specific language, real outcomes, and a human voice are competitive advantages.
