# Content Writing Frameworks & Best Practices

A research-grounded reference for structuring persuasive, authoritative, and
human content. These frameworks are not interchangeable templates — each maps
to a specific audience awareness level, content goal, and psychological mechanism.
Using the wrong framework for the context is worse than using no framework at all.

---

## How to choose a framework

Before reaching for a framework, answer two questions:

1. **Where is my audience in their awareness?**
   Cold audiences (unaware of the problem) → AIDA, StoryBrand
   Problem-aware audiences → PAS, PASTOR, 4Ps
   Solution-aware audiences → BAB, FAB, SCQA
   Product-aware audiences → FAB, 4Cs

2. **What is the primary goal?**
   Convert to purchase/lead → AIDA, PAS, PASTOR, CONVERT
   Build authority and trust → Thought Leadership, LEMA, SCQA
   Create emotional connection → StoryBrand, BAB, Thought Companionship
   Inform and educate → SCQA, 4-Point, LEMA
   Demonstrate transformation → BAB, PASTOR

**Critical warning:** Research confirms that overusing any single framework
breeds predictability. 60–80% of effective copywriting is audience research —
frameworks structure the delivery, they don't replace understanding the reader.

---

## Part 1: Conversion Frameworks

These frameworks are built to move readers from awareness to action.

---

### 1. AIDA

**Best for:** Ads, landing pages, email campaigns, cold outreach
**Audience state:** Unaware to problem-aware
**Psychological mechanism:** Mirrors the natural cognitive sequence from
attention to decision

**Structure:**

- **Attention** — Stop the scroll. One unexpected angle, specific number,
  or provocative claim. Not a generic headline.
- **Interest** — Build relevance by connecting to what they already care about.
  Fresh, counter-intuitive, or useful information.
- **Desire** — Show the transformation. Benefits, not features. Emotion first,
  logic to justify.
- **Action** — One specific, friction-reduced next step.

**Critical limitation:** AIDA has no built-in trust or proof step. Modern
buyers research before deciding. In high-skepticism contexts, add a Conviction
step between Desire and Action (making it AIDCA) — present testimonials,
data, or a credible guarantee before the ask.

**When to use:** Short-form conversion: ads, email subject lines, social posts,
product announcements, awareness campaigns. When speed of conversion matters
more than depth of persuasion.

**When NOT to use:** High-ticket B2B, long sales cycles, technical audiences
who evaluate before deciding. AIDA feels thin when the stakes are high.

**Example pattern:**
> Attention: Specific, surprising headline
> Interest: The counterintuitive reality of the problem
> Desire: What changes after the solution + one proof point
> Action: Single, specific CTA with friction removed

---

### 2. PAS (Problem → Agitate → Solution)

**Best for:** Blog intros, email sequences, landing pages, social posts
**Audience state:** Problem-aware — they know the pain, haven't found the fix
**Psychological mechanism:** Pain precedes search for relief — a consistent
psychological driver. PAS is experiencing the strongest momentum in 2024–2025
precisely because it bypasses the attention economy: starting with the reader's
problem skips the need to "earn" their attention.

**Structure:**

- **Problem** — Name the specific pain in the audience's own language, not
  your interpretation of it. Pull from reviews, support tickets, sales calls.
  Generic problems don't convert.
- **Agitate** — Show the consequences of inaction and the emotional weight
  of living with the problem. What does it cost them in time, money, status,
  or peace of mind? Do not manufacture fear — stay grounded in what's actually
  true. Exaggerated agitation loses credibility.
- **Solution** — Present the path forward. Not a feature list — a clear,
  believable way out. Your offer as the relief.

**Modern variant — PASO:** Add an Outcome step after Solution. Let the reader
see the specific transformed state, not just the solution mechanism. PAS tells
them the problem is solved; PASO shows them what solved looks like.

**When to use:** Any content where the reader is already feeling a pain. Email
campaigns, landing pages, organic social, blog intros for problem-focused posts.

**When NOT to use:** Audiences who aren't problem-aware yet (use AIDA first).
High-context, high-trust cultures where fear-based amplification can backfire.

**Example pattern:**
> Problem: "Every report takes your team four hours to build..."
> Agitate: "That's 16 hours a week per analyst — a full two days of manual
> work that could be client-facing time. And one wrong formula sinks the deck."
> Solution: "X connects directly to your data sources and builds the report
> in under eight minutes, automatically."

---

### 3. BAB (Before → After → Bridge)

**Best for:** Case studies, testimonials, transformation content, social proof
**Audience state:** Problem-aware, looking for evidence of transformation
**Psychological mechanism:** People make decisions by comparing current state
to desired future state — BAB makes that comparison explicit

**Structure:**

- **Before** — Paint the specific, relatable picture of life with the problem.
  The messier and more honest, the better.
- **After** — Show the transformed state in concrete terms. Numbers, time
  saved, stress removed, opportunity captured. Not vague improvement — specific
  outcome.
- **Bridge** — How does the transformation happen? Your product, service, or
  framework is the bridge between the two states.

**When to use:** Case study copy, testimonials, about page narratives, social
content showcasing results, retargeting ads showing what customers achieved.

**When NOT to use:** Cold audiences who haven't established the "before" in
their own mind yet. Serve them AIDA or PAS first.

**Critical execution note:** The After must be specific enough to be
believable and aspirational enough to be desired. "We saved time" is Before
copy. "We cut monthly reporting from 12 hours to 90 minutes" is After copy.

---

### 4. FAB (Features → Advantages → Benefits)

**Best for:** Product descriptions, service pages, technical content, proposals
**Audience state:** Solution-aware — they know the solution category, need to
choose between options
**Psychological mechanism:** Bridges rational product information to emotional
buyer motivation; closes the gap between what a product is and why they care

**Structure:**

- **Features** — What it is. The objective facts.
- **Advantages** — What it does. How it works differently from alternatives.
- **Benefits** — What it means for them. The outcome in their world.

**Execution rule — always complete the chain:**
> "AI-powered routing [Feature] automatically sends leads to the right rep
> based on territory and deal size [Advantage], so your top performers stop
> being buried in low-value leads [Benefit]."

**When to use:** Sales proposals, product pages, service descriptions,
pitch decks — anywhere buyers are evaluating options and need both rational
and emotional justification.

**When NOT to use:** Cold audiences who haven't accepted they have a problem
yet. FAB only works when someone already wants the solution category.

---

### 5. PASTOR (Person/Problem/Pain → Amplify → Story/Solution → Transformation → Offer → Response)

**Best for:** Long-form sales pages, email sequences, proposals, webinar scripts
**Audience state:** Problem-aware to solution-aware
**Creator:** Ray Edwards
**Psychological mechanism:** The most complete conversion framework — covers
empathy (P), urgency (A), credibility (S), proof (T), value (O), and commitment
(R) in a single structure. Unlike PAS, PASTOR explicitly builds transformation
evidence (testimonials) before the offer.

**Structure:**

- **Person / Problem / Pain** — Identify who you're talking to, their specific
  problem, and the pain that problem causes. Empathize first; don't sound
  salesy. You're a shepherd, not a closer.
- **Amplify** — Show the specific cost of not solving the problem. Both the
  downside (what continues if they don't act) and the aspirational pain (what
  they wish they had that your solution provides).
- **Story / Solution** — A relatable story that models the transformation,
  or a clear presentation of how the solution works. This is where credibility
  is built — not claimed.
- **Transformation / Testimony** — What does life look like after? Anchored
  in real testimonials with specific outcomes. People don't buy products;
  they buy the transformation the product produces.
- **Offer** — Present what's included, clearly and specifically. The offer
  is not a pitch — it's a clear description of the vehicle that produces the
  transformation.
- **Response** — Call to action that removes doubt, addresses objections,
  and makes the next step obvious. Not "buy now" — the specific, friction-free
  path forward.

**When to use:** Full sales pages, VSLs, detailed email sequences, high-ticket
consulting proposals. Any context where you need to earn trust, demonstrate
transformation, and close — not just convert.

**When NOT to use:** Short-form content where the full structure can't breathe.
Condense PASTOR to P-A-S for blog posts and social.

---

### 6. 4Ps (Problem → Promise → Proof → Proposal)

**Best for:** Email campaigns, sales letters, landing pages, short pitch sequences
**Audience state:** Problem-aware
**Psychological mechanism:** Rational persuasion — addresses skepticism
through evidence rather than emotion alone

**Structure:**

- **Problem** — Name the specific problem your audience faces. Frame it in
  their language.
- **Promise** — Make a clear, specific, believable promise about what changes.
- **Proof** — Validate the promise with evidence: data, case studies,
  testimonials, specific metrics. This is the trust layer most short frameworks
  skip.
- **Proposal** — The clear, low-friction next step. What you're offering and
  what you want them to do.

**Note on variants:** You'll encounter 4Ps as both "Problem/Promise/Proof/Proposal"
and "Picture/Promise/Proof/Push." Both work. The Picture variant leads with a
vivid scene rather than naming a problem directly — effective when you want
to let the reader arrive at the problem themselves.

**When to use:** Email campaigns to warm lists, sales letters, retargeting,
B2B prospect sequences where skepticism is the primary barrier.

---

### 7. CONVERT

**Best for:** Comprehensive conversion-focused pages, lead generation
**Audience state:** Any — CONVERT is a quality filter applied after the primary
framework is chosen
**Psychological mechanism:** Ensures all seven persuasion dimensions are
present before publishing

**Structure:**

- **Clarity** — Can a first-time reader understand this in one pass?
- **Outcome** — Is the end result of the offer visible and specific?
- **Narrative** — Does the content flow as a story, not a list of claims?
- **Value** — Is the value proposition concrete and unique?
- **Engagement** — Is there a hook or question that earns continued reading?
- **Relevance** — Does every section speak directly to the stated audience?
- **Trigger** — Is there one clear, compelling call to action?

**When to use:** Run any conversion-focused draft through CONVERT as a
checklist after writing with another framework. It catches missing elements
before publishing.

## Part 2: Authority & Analytical Frameworks

These frameworks are built for content where authority, logical clarity,
or structured analysis is the primary goal.

---

### 8. SCQA (Situation → Complication → Question → Answer)

**Best for:** Executive communications, B2B thought pieces, white papers,
analytical articles, consulting proposals, any content where the audience is
analytical and time-limited
**Creator:** Barbara Minto, McKinsey & Company (from *The Pyramid Principle*)
**Psychological mechanism:** The most respected business communication framework
in the world — McKinsey, BCG, and Bain consultants use it for every executive
summary. It mirrors how decision-makers process complex information: context
first, tension second, resolution last.

**Structure:**

- **Situation** — Establish the stable, agreed-upon context. What does
  everyone already know and accept as true? This earns trust before tension.
- **Complication** — What has changed or what problem has arisen that
  disrupts the situation? This is why the audience is reading — the reason
  the document exists.
- **Question** — Make the core question explicit. What specifically needs
  to be resolved? (In some implementations, this is implied rather than
  stated.)
- **Answer** — Lead with the conclusion, then support it. Unlike most
  narrative writing, SCQA states the answer up front and proves it
  afterward. This is the key inversion that makes it so effective for
  analytical audiences — they don't have to read to the end to know what
  you think.

**The Minto Pyramid Principle connection:** SCQA structures the introduction.
The rest of the document proves the Answer with logically grouped arguments,
each supported by data. The structure resembles a pyramid: main idea at the
top, supporting arguments below.

**When to use:** Executive briefings, white papers, consulting deliverables,
investor memos, case studies for analytical buyers, LinkedIn articles aimed at
decision-makers. Any context where the reader is pressed for time and high on
skepticism.

**When NOT to use:** Emotional or brand storytelling. SCQA is a logic engine
— it doesn't create desire, it satisfies rational requirements. Pair it with
BAB or StoryBrand when emotion matters.

**Example pattern:**
> Situation: "B2B buying decisions now involve an average of 6–10 stakeholders..."
> Complication: "Despite this, most vendor content is written for the primary
> buyer — leaving the 40%+ of deals that stall due to internal misalignment
> unaddressed."
> Question: "How should B2B content strategy evolve to reach the full buying group?"
> Answer: "Lead with thought leadership that addresses cross-functional
> concerns, not just technical evaluation criteria — here's the evidence."

---

### 9. LEMA

**Best for:** Authority articles, thought leadership, educational content,
long-form essays
**Creator:** Lily Ugbaja
**Psychological mechanism:** Makes content cognitively sticky — ideas that
flow logically (Logic) are absorbed, ideas in concrete language (Explicitness)
are trusted, ideas wrapped in memorable structures (Memorability) are retained,
and ideas with clear next steps (Actionability) drive behavior.

**Structure:**

- **Logic** — Ideas flow naturally from one to the next. Every section
  answers the question the previous one raises. The reader is never left
  wondering "so what?"
- **Explicitness** — Concrete and specific throughout. No vague claims,
  no ambiguous language. Every general statement is followed by a specific
  example, number, or illustration.
- **Memorability** — Coin terms, use unexpected analogies, introduce a
  framework or metaphor that makes the central idea stick. If a reader
  can't explain the article's main insight to someone else in one sentence,
  it wasn't memorable enough.
- **Actionability** — Clear next steps that a reader can take immediately.
  Pathway from insight to application.

**When to use:** TOFU thought leadership, newsletter essays, industry reports,
educational blog posts where authority and retention matter more than immediate
conversion. LEMA is why readers share content — it gives them something to
say about what they just read.

---

### 10. 4-Point Content Framework

**Best for:** Long-form articles, blog posts, guides — any content where
engagement and dwell time are primary metrics
**Creator:** Neil Patel
**Psychological mechanism:** Addresses the full reading lifecycle, from
first impression to final impression

**Structure:**

1. **Hook Attention Early** — A specific, counterintuitive, or stakes-raising
   opening that earns the right to the next sentence. Headlines and first lines
   get 80% of reader decisions. Spend disproportionate time here.
2. **Make It Effortless to Read** — Formatting, subheadings, whitespace,
   paragraph length. Content that is hard to read doesn't get read.
   73% of internet readers explicitly say they need scannable content.
3. **Maintain Narrative Flow** — Smooth transitions, logical progression,
   no sudden topic jumps. Every section earns the right to the next one.
4. **Polish Ruthlessly** — Cut anything that doesn't earn its place. Strengthen
   the CTA. Add one finishing touch that makes the ending memorable.

**When to use:** Any long-form content production process as a quality filter
at the end of drafting. Apply as a revision checklist, not as an initial
structure.

## Part 3: Storytelling Frameworks

These frameworks use narrative structure to create identification and trust.

---

### 11. StoryBrand (SB7)

**Best for:** Brand messaging, website copy, marketing strategy, any content
where the customer relationship is the primary asset
**Creator:** Donald Miller (*Building a StoryBrand*, 2017; *StoryBrand 2.0*, January 2025)
**Psychological mechanism:** Human cognition processes narrative more efficiently
than feature lists. By positioning the customer as the hero and the brand as
the guide, SB7 inverts the self-centered marketing that most brands produce —
and which buyers have learned to tune out.

**Structure (the 7 elements):**

1. **A Character** — The customer is the hero. Not the company. Define who
   they are and what they want.
2. **Has a Problem** — Three levels of problem:
   - *External*: The tangible, observable problem
   - *Internal*: The frustration, doubt, or inadequacy they feel because of it
   - *Philosophical*: Why it's wrong that this problem exists
3. **Meets a Guide** — The brand enters as a guide — not a hero. The guide
   has two things: *empathy* (I understand your problem) and *authority*
   (I've helped others solve it). Over-claiming authority without empathy
   makes the brand seem arrogant. Empathy alone makes it seem weak.
4. **Who Gives Them a Plan** — A clear, simple process that shows how
   the problem gets solved. Simplicity reduces confusion; confusion is the
   death of conversion.
5. **Calls Them to Action** — A direct CTA (what to do now) and a
   transitional CTA (not ready to buy? here's a lower-commitment step).
6. **Helps Them Avoid Failure** — Stakes: what does the hero lose if they
   don't act? Make this real, not manipulative. Show what continuing without
   the solution costs.
7. **Ends in Success** — What the hero's life looks like after. Specific,
   desirable, achieved.

**Why it matters now:** StoryBrand 2.0 (January 2025) clarifies the framework
for digital-first applications. Marketing copy that follows story structure
converts better than feature catalogs because narrative creates cause-and-effect
clarity that lists cannot.

**When to use:** Website messaging architecture, brand positioning, homepage
copy, any context where you need to define your brand's relationship to customers.

**When NOT to use:** Direct response copy with warm audiences. StoryBrand is
a positioning framework, not a conversion framework. Pair it with PAS or PASTOR
for the actual conversion layer.

## Part 4: Universal Quality Frameworks

These aren't structural frameworks — they're quality standards applied to
any content regardless of the primary framework used.

---

### 12. The 4 C's

**Purpose:** Quality checklist applied to any draft before publishing

- **Clear** — A reader encounters the content for the first time. Do they
  understand the core point without effort?
- **Concise** — Every word earns its place. Cut anything that doesn't
  add information or move the reader forward.
- **Compelling** — Does the content hold attention from start to finish?
  Is there a reason to keep reading?
- **Credible** — Are claims backed by specific evidence? Is the author
  or brand's authority established?

## Part 5: Content Pillars

Two content modes that are not frameworks but strategies — they shape why
you write and how you position content in the reader's life.

---

### Pillar A: Thought Leadership

**What it is:** Content that earns authority by demonstrating expertise through
original insight, not by describing your product or service. The distinction
matters: thought leadership is free, ungated, and audience-serving. It does
not describe your capabilities — it demonstrates them.

**Why it matters (the data):**
The 2025 Edelman-LinkedIn B2B Thought Leadership Impact Report (nearly 2,000
global professionals) found:

- **71%** of hidden decision-makers (finance, legal, compliance, operations —
  people who influence purchases but don't appear in your CRM) believe thought
  leadership is more effective than conventional marketing at demonstrating
  vendor capabilities
- **64%** trust thought leadership content more than marketing materials when
  assessing competencies
- **79%** of hidden buyers are more likely to support proposals from vendors
  who consistently publish strong thought leadership
- **95%** of hidden buyers say strong thought leadership makes them more
  receptive to outreach
- Over **40%** of B2B deals stall due to internal misalignment within buying
  groups — thought leadership reaches these internal influencers when sales
  and marketing cannot

**What makes thought leadership work:**
Thought leadership is not expert blog posts. The difference:

| Thought leadership | Expert blog posts |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| A point of view the author defends | Summary of what others have said |
| Original data, research, or synthesis | Curated information |
| Challenges the audience's assumptions | Confirms what they already think |
| Advances industry conversation | Covers existing ground |
| Bold claim the author stands behind | Balanced overview with no position |

**Formats that work for thought leadership:**

- Original research reports with proprietary data
- Contrarian essays that defend an unpopular position with evidence
- Playbooks and frameworks the author invented from experience
- In-depth analysis of industry data others have collected but not interpreted
- Founder/executive personal essays from lived experience in the industry

**What fails:**

- "10 tips" posts — this is expert content, not thought leadership
- Vendor-neutral overviews of a topic your audience already knows
- Opinions without supporting evidence
- Articles that don't take a position

**Writing a thought leadership piece:**

1. Start with a genuine belief about your industry that most people would
   initially disagree with or haven't yet articulated
2. Find or commission data that either supports or complicates your belief
3. Write the argument, not the information — defend your position
4. Make it specific to your industry context, not generic
5. End with the implication: if I'm right about this, what should you do?

**Frameworks that pair with thought leadership:**
LEMA (for structure and memorability), SCQA (for analytical audiences),
4-Point (for long-form engagement)

---

### Pillar B: Thought Companionship

**What it is:** A content strategy and voice orientation in which the writer
positions themselves not as an expert delivering information, but as a thinking
partner working through ideas alongside the reader. Where thought leadership
says "here is what you should know," thought companionship says "here is what
I'm figuring out — let's think through it together."

**The psychological foundation:**
Parasocial relationships — the one-sided but emotionally real connections
that audiences form with media figures — are built on companionship. Research
shows companionship is a primary motivation for media use: audiences develop
bonds with writers, hosts, and creators that fulfill genuine human needs for
intellectual connection, belonging, and shared exploration.

The key mechanism: when a writer invites the reader into their genuine
uncertainty or curiosity — rather than performing expertise — it creates
a fundamentally different relationship than authority-based content. The reader
stops feeling informed and starts feeling understood.

**Why this is strategically distinct from thought leadership:**
Thought leadership earns attention by being right.
Thought companionship earns loyalty by being real.

A brand or individual can do both — they operate in different content contexts:

| Thought leadership | Thought companionship |
|-------------------|--------------------|
| Establishes authority | Builds intimacy |
| Defends a position | Explores a question |
| Reader learns from you | Reader thinks with you |
| Best for: acquisition, credibility | Best for: retention, community, newsletter |
| Metric: new audiences reached | Metric: open rates, replies, depth of engagement |

**What thought companionship looks like in practice:**

*In newsletters:*

- Sharing a question you don't have the answer to yet
- Documenting a change in your thinking and explaining why
- Working through a complex decision in real time
- Naming the tension between two things you believe that seem to conflict

*In essays:*

- Using "I" naturally — not as performance of authenticity, but because
  you are genuinely present in the exploration
- Acknowledging when your position has evolved since the last time you wrote
  about this
- Letting some questions stay open rather than resolving everything into
  clean takeaways
- Writing about the process of figuring something out, not just the conclusion

*In social content:*

- Sharing what you're currently wrestling with, not just what you've concluded
- Asking genuine questions (not engagement-bait questions)
- Responding to replies and letting them change the next piece you write
- Occasional vulnerability about the limits of your own expertise

**What it is not:**

- False vulnerability — manufactured admission of weakness designed to seem
  relatable. Readers detect this immediately. Real companionship is specific;
  performed companionship is vague.
- Thinking out loud without purpose — rambling without a thread
- Declining to take positions — thought companionship still has a perspective;
  it just holds it with more openness
- Replacing expertise — the most effective thought companions are highly
  knowledgeable people who are willing to publicly think. The knowledge is the
  floor, not the ceiling.

**Frameworks that pair with thought companionship:**
4-Point (for structure), LEMA (for memorability), SCQA (for analytical threads)

**How to write in thought companionship mode:**

1. Start with a genuine question you're actually thinking about
2. Share what you know, what you're uncertain about, and what you've changed
   your mind on
3. Invite the reader into the complexity rather than resolving it for them
4. End with a concrete thought or observation — not a lesson, but a point
   of honest arrival
5. Leave space for the reader to respond, disagree, or carry the thread

## Part 6: Platform-Specific Conventions

### LinkedIn

**Optimal post length (2025–2026 data):**
1,200–1,600 characters for standard feed posts. Posts under 500 characters
are classified as low-effort by the algorithm and receive significantly reduced
distribution. Posts over 2,500 characters see completion rates drop.

**The fold problem:** Only the first 210 characters appear before "See more."
60–70% of readers never click to expand. The first two lines are the only
two lines most readers see. Write them accordingly.

**Algorithm context (2025–2026):** LinkedIn rebuilt its feed algorithm to
prioritize "knowledge and advice" content from subject matter experts.
Engagement pods, hashtag stuffing, and "agree?" posts now actively hurt reach.
The algorithm rewards dwell time, engagement velocity, and completion rate —
all of which are driven by actual content quality, not distribution tactics.

**Format guidance:**

- Text posts: 1,200–1,600 characters
- Line breaks every 2–3 lines for mobile readability (70% of LinkedIn traffic
  is mobile)
- Links: first comment only (link in post body suppresses distribution)
- Hashtags: 3–5, specific to your industry, placed at the end
- Carousels: highest engagement format (6.6% vs. 5.6% for video)

**Structure:**

- Hook (first 2 lines): specific observation, counterintuitive claim, or
  concrete number — must earn the "see more" click
- Value delivery: the insight, story, or argument
- CTA or question: invite response or point to next step

---

### Twitter / X

**Format guidance:**

- Single tweets: one idea, one point, maximum 280 characters
- Threads: 5–12 tweets for most topics; numbered (1/10) for discoverability
- Hook tweet is everything — the first tweet determines whether anyone reads
  the thread
- Links: final tweet only
- No link in tweet body on single posts — it suppresses distribution

**Structure for threads:** Hook → Build (expanding context, evidence, examples)
→ Payoff (the specific insight or conclusion) → CTA (optional, in final tweet)

---

### Instagram

**Caption guidance:**

- First 125 characters are visible before "more" — front-load the hook
- Short captions (under 150 characters): work for images that speak for themselves
- Long captions (1,000–2,200 characters): work for storytelling content,
  personal essays, behind-the-scenes narratives
- Hashtags: 3–5 in caption or first comment; over-hashtagging reduces credibility
- CTAs: "link in bio" is the primary mechanism; be specific about what's there

**Structure:** Hook (first 125 characters) → Story or value delivery → CTA

---

### Facebook

**Format guidance:**

- Conversational, community-focused tone
- 100–250 characters for maximum organic reach on simple posts
- Link in first comment for better algorithmic performance on link-sharing
- Ask questions or invite community response to drive engagement

## Part 8: Voice Calibration

### Analyzing voice from existing content

When scanning URLs or writing samples to calibrate brand voice, assess:

**Sentence structure:**

- Average sentence length and range
- Mix of simple vs. complex sentences
- Whether rhythm varies or stays uniform (uniform = AI tell)

**Vocabulary:**

- Technical vs. casual register
- Industry jargon: how much, how dense
- Unique phrases, coined terms, or recurring expressions

**Tone markers:**

- Formal vs. informal
- Direct vs. diplomatic
- Confident vs. hedging
- Presence or absence of humor

**Personality indicators:**

- Use of first person
- Opinions stated as opinions
- Willingness to take positions others might disagree with
- Self-awareness or reflection about limitations

**Formatting preferences:**

- Paragraph length
- Use of lists vs. prose
- Subheading style (questions vs. statements)
- Bold, italics, and emphasis patterns

## Part 9: Content Generation Process

### Phase 1: Discuss (Requirements)

Before selecting any framework, answer:

- What's the topic and angle?
- Who specifically is the audience and where are they in their awareness?
- What's the primary goal: awareness, trust, conversion, retention?
- Which framework fits this goal and audience state?
- What tone and register?
- What platform and format?
- What CTA or next step?

**Selection trap to avoid:** Defaulting to the same framework for every piece.
If you always write PAS, you're not calibrating to the audience — you're
applying a favorite template.

### Phase 2: Plan (Research & Strategy)

- Research the topic and identify what's already been said
- Find the angle that hasn't been taken or the data that challenges the
  conventional view
- Select framework
- Outline sections with the framework structure made explicit
- Identify examples, data points, and proof elements for each section
- Define SEO strategy if relevant

### Phase 3: Execute (Write)

- Apply the framework — but don't follow it mechanically. The framework
  shapes structure; the voice shapes execution.
- Write with specificity: names, numbers, dates, real examples
- Vary sentence length and structure — burstiness is a human signature
- Maintain consistent brand voice throughout
- Follow platform conventions

### Phase 4: Verify (Quality Check)

- Run anti-AI audit (humanizer)
- Check SEO if applicable (claude-seo)
- Apply 4Cs or CONVERT checklist
- Verify the framework is working (is each section doing its job?)
- Read aloud — if it sounds like no one in particular, it needs more voice
- Proofread

### Phase 5: Ship (Finalize)

- Save to appropriate directory
- Generate metadata if required
- Platform-specific formatting check
- Publishing notes

## Part 10: Anti-AI Writing Principles

### Patterns to eliminate

**Structural:**

- Parallel list structures with identical grammar
- Rule of three everywhere (use two, or four, or one elaborated point)
- Triple anaphora ("We can't... We can't... We can't...")
- Negative parallelism ("It's not X — it's Y")
- Throat-clearing openers ("In today's fast-paced world...")
- Formulaic challenge-and-resolution ("Despite these challenges...")
- Signposting transitions ("It's worth noting that...")

**Word-level:**

- High-frequency AI vocabulary: additionally, align with, boasts, breathtaking,
  certainly, comprehensive, crucial, cutting-edge, delve, elevate, emphasizing,
  enhance, enduring, foster, groundbreaking, holistic, innovative, intricate,
  key (as adj.), landscape (abstract), leverage (verb), meticulous, nestled,
  paradigm, pivotal, robust, seamless, showcase, streamline, stunning, synergy,
  tapestry, testament, transformative, underscore, utilize, valuable, vibrant
- Em dash overuse (cap at 2–3 per article)
- Bold overuse in running prose

**Structural tells:**

- Symmetric section treatment (all sections same length)
- Inline-header bullet lists ("**Performance:** Performance has been improved...")
- Generic positive conclusions ("The future looks bright...")
- Section-ending summaries ("As we've seen...")

### Write like a human

- **Have opinions and state them as yours.** "I don't know how to feel about
  this" is more human than a balanced pro/con list.
- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Short. Then a longer one that works
  through a secondary point before landing. The variance is a biological
  fingerprint.
- **Be specific about uncertainty.** "I'm not sure this will hold long-term
  because X" beats "while there are challenges..."
- **Use first person when appropriate.** Not as performance — as presence.
- **Dwell on what's interesting, move fast through what's not.** Equal
  treatment of all sections is an AI signature.
- **Use specifics no model would default to.** Dollar amounts, dates, real
  names, a product version number, a company that no longer exists.

## Part 11: SEO Integration

### On-page checklist

- [ ] Primary keyword in title, naturally (not forced)
- [ ] Primary keyword in first 100 words
- [ ] Keyword variations throughout (semantic density, not stuffing)
- [ ] H2/H3 subheadings with descriptive language
- [ ] Internal links to related content
- [ ] External links to authoritative sources
- [ ] Meta title (50–60 characters)
- [ ] Meta description (140–160 characters)
- [ ] Image alt text
- [ ] URL slug: short, keyword-relevant, lowercase with hyphens

### Content SEO principles

- Write for the human reader first; optimize for search second
- Answer the search intent completely — the question behind the query
- Include related entities and topics (NLP optimization)
- Structure for AI citation: self-contained paragraphs that answer implied
  questions, FAQ sections with FAQ schema for AI Overview inclusion

## Part 12: Quality Checklist

### Before publishing

**Content:**

- [ ] Achieves the stated goal
- [ ] Uses the appropriate framework for this audience and purpose
- [ ] Contains specific examples, numbers, or real evidence
- [ ] No AI writing patterns (refer to Part 10)
- [ ] Maintains consistent brand voice throughout

**Structure:**

- [ ] Opens with something that earns the next sentence
- [ ] Each section serves a clear function in the framework
- [ ] Transitions between sections are smooth and earned
- [ ] Closes with a concrete point, action, or observation — not vague inspiration

**Technical:**

- [ ] No spelling or grammar errors
- [ ] Formatted correctly for the platform
- [ ] SEO elements present and accurate (if applicable)
- [ ] Links verified

**Persuasion:**

- [ ] Value proposition is concrete and specific
- [ ] Social proof is present and attributed
- [ ] Objections are addressed (explicitly or implicitly)
- [ ] CTA is clear, single, and friction-reduced
